\chapter{Eternal Dawn}
\label{chap:dawn}

The void decays into seeds (Chapter~\ref{chap:void}), and each seed bounces
(Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce}) into a world. A world born this way---directly from
the void, with no parent outside it---is an \emph{original-generation universe},
an OGU: the first dawn of a lineage. This chapter asks what those first universes
are like---what they inherit, how they choose between matter and antimatter, what
sizes and spins they come in---and how, by growing old and dying, each one seeds
the next, so that the dawn is not a single event but an eternal, recurring one.

\section{The first dawn, and what a universe inherits}
\label{sec:first-dawn}

Just past the bounce the new universe is at its densest, and the torsion
repulsion that halted the collapse is still near its peak. It drives a brief
epoch of very rapid expansion that fades as the density drops and gravity
reasserts itself---an inflation-like phase with no separate inflaton field,
ending on its own when the density falls below the bounce threshold, and leaving
behind the homogeneous, near-isotropic conditions a hot cosmology needs. Deriving
its perturbation spectrum and matching it to the microwave sky is the central
near-term test of the whole picture (Chapter~\ref{chap:nowobs}).

What actually comes through the membrane is set by conservation laws, not by
inheritance of any structure. Energy--momentum is conserved, fixing the new
universe's mass budget. Net angular momentum passes through, so a rotating seed or
parent leaves the child with a \emph{preferred axis}---a candidate source of the
large-scale anisotropies discussed later. The conserved charges flow through, in
particular the combination $B-L$ that will carry the matter excess. What does
\emph{not} come through is everything made of structure: atoms, nuclei, particle
identities, any record of a ``before.'' All of it is dissolved at the bounce
density and rebuilt from elementary field content on the far side
(Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce}). A universe inherits a budget and a few conserved
numbers, not a cargo.

\section{The matter choice}
\label{sec:matter-choice}

One of those conserved numbers is the most consequential the universe will carry:
the slight excess of matter over antimatter, measured today as the
baryon-to-photon ratio $\eta\simeq6\times10^{-10}$, about 0.6 parts per billion.
Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce} established that the bounce cannot \emph{make} this
excess---the interaction that drives it is $C$-, $P$-, and $T$-even, so it neither
flips charge nor generates a chiral bias from nothing. The asymmetry must be
\emph{supplied}. The framework's account of where it comes from divides cleanly by
generation.

\paragraph{For a child universe: inheritance, essentially exact.} A universe with
a parent inherits the parent's excess directly. At the bounce the infalling
material thermalises at $\sim10^{20}\,\mathrm{K}$ into a near-symmetric bath in
which every species is pair-produced; the parent's particular matter is erased.
What survives is whatever net $B-L$ the material carried, because the electroweak
sphalerons active at the bounce temperature wash out $B+L$ but cannot touch
$B-L$. The crossing is also fast---$\sim4\times10^{-21}\,\mathrm{s}$, far shorter
than any thermalisation time---so it generates essentially no entropy
(the dilution factor $D=S_{\mathrm{after}}/S_{\mathrm{before}}=1$ to about one part
in $10^{11}$; Chapter~\ref{chap:sims}). With entropy conserved, the child does not
regenerate its photons, and so it inherits the parent's ratio outright,
$\eta_{\mathrm{child}}\approx\eta_{\mathrm{parent}}$. The excess is passed down a
lineage like a family name, not re-manufactured at each step.

\paragraph{For the first universe: the seed's lucky skew.} Only the OGU, with no
parent, must source its excess at birth---and even there nothing needs to
\emph{make} it. A vacuum fluctuation rare enough to nucleate a bounce is not
exactly matter--antimatter symmetric; it carries some chance net $B-L$. The
symmetric part incinerates in the thermal bath, and the surviving skew fixes that
lineage's $\eta$. A quantitative attempt to source the skew dynamically---the
chiral-vortical effect, in which the rotating bounce's vorticity $\omega$ drives an
axial current and freezes in a bias $\eta\sim C\,(\omega/T)$---gives the right
\emph{order} but not, yet, the right number: at the bounce density the bare,
parameter-free ratio is $\omega/T\sim1.5\times10^{-11}$, landing within about 1.6
orders of magnitude of $\eta_{\mathrm{obs}}$ when it could as easily have been
$10^{-30}$, but matching the value would require an uncomputed coefficient
$C\!\cdot\!f_\omega\simeq41$. This is a scaling estimate with explicit
assumptions, not a derivation (reproducible as
\texttt{sims/src/cartasis\_sims/cve\_filter.py}), and it binds \emph{only} the
OGU; for the rest of us the excess is inherited debris, and the shortfall is not
our problem.

\paragraph{The needle, threaded by selection.} The surviving excess must thread a
fine target: too much and the universe is over-baryonic, too little and it
annihilates to a sterile furnace; the viable window sits at about one part per
billion. The framework does not fine-tune to hit it---it \emph{selects}. Only
lineages founded on a sufficiently skewed seed are matter-rich enough to make
stars, black holes, and descendants at all (\S\ref{sec:shape}); the barren ones
make nothing and are never anyone's parent. We carry down what a viable founder
happened to have. What remains genuinely open is not the stability of $\eta$ along
a lineage but its \emph{value} at the founder---why $\sim10^{-9}$ rather than
$10^{-3}$ or $10^{-15}$ (see the appendix).

\paragraph{Why matter comes paired with a forward arrow.} The thermodynamic arrow
of Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce} is set by the low-entropy membrane and would exist
even in a perfectly symmetric universe; it is not \emph{caused} by the matter
excess. But the two are co-sourced at the same surface and locked together across
the supraverse. The process that biases the matter is $CP$-violating, and by the
$CPT$ theorem $CP$ violation is $T$ violation, so matter genesis is intrinsically
time-asymmetric. Because the void is $CPT$-symmetric, for every matter-led
universe running forward there is a $CPT$-image partner that is antimatter-led and
runs the opposite way---the same object seen through $CPT$. The quantity fixed by
symmetry is the \emph{product} of matter-sign and arrow-sign, and observership
selects the diagonal: every observer necessarily finds ``matter, forward in
time,'' while the inhabitants of the mirror lineage say exactly the same of their
``antimatter, backward'' world. Matter and the arrow travel together not because
one causes the other, but because the $CPT$ structure of the void binds their
signs.

\section{The shape of a generation}
\label{sec:shape}

A generation of first universes is not uniform; it has a derivable distribution in
both size and spin, and we should expect to find ourselves at its typical point,
not in its rare tail.

\paragraph{Size: small is typical.} Two pressures compete. Births favour the
small---a fluctuation is exponentially less likely to reach the bounce density
over a larger mass. Growth favours the large---once born, a hole feeds by runaway
accretion. In the stationary population these balance into a power law,
$n(M)\propto M^{-g}$ (with $g\simeq1$--$2$ for Eddington- to Bondi-limited
feeding): many small universes, few large, cut off at the high-mass end
(\Cref{fig:ogudist}, left). Since the count falls with mass, a low-mass viability
cut---enough mass to make stars and black holes---selects observer-bearing
universes that pile up just above the edge. The old conjecture that ``we sit near
the optimum'' is thus \emph{derived}: a typical observer is near the viability edge
of a declining power law, not out in the exponentially rare massive tail.

\paragraph{Spin: barely viable is typical, and that is why $\eta$ is small.} The
seed's net vorticity sets the inherited bias, $|\eta|\sim C\,|\omega/T|$. A random
fluctuation most likely has \emph{small} net vorticity, but a seed below
$\omega_{\min}=\eta_{\min}T/C$ cannot complete baryogenesis: its matter and
antimatter annihilate to near-symmetric radiation, no structure forms, and the
universe is sterile. Viability therefore gates the distribution from below, and the
result runs exactly counter to intuition (\Cref{fig:ogudist}, right): \emph{no
spin is likeliest but most sterile, high spin is rare but pure}, and the typical
\emph{viable} universe sits just above the threshold---low-spin, low-purity,
barely past barren. This is a shape, not yet a number (it is plotted in units of an
unknown birth-vorticity scale), but it carries a real payoff: it explains why the
cosmic matter asymmetry is \emph{small without fine-tuning}. A small $\eta$ is what
a typical viable universe has. The same low spin predicts a small but nonzero net
rotation for our universe---a preferred axis to confront with galaxy-spin
handedness and CMB alignments (Chapter~\ref{chap:nowobs}).

\paragraph{Lineages are chirally pure.} Because the sign of the excess is set once,
at the seed, and merely passed down thereafter, a matter-led OGU has only matter
descendants and an antimatter-led one only antimatter, with no mixing. The vacuum's
$CPT$ symmetry makes the two kinds equally numerous, so global $CPT$ is preserved
even though every individual lineage is pure. What is inherited is the chirality
\emph{sign} alone---\emph{not} the spin: a descendant's rotation axis and magnitude
are its own progenitor hole's, set by a local astrophysical collapse and re-drawn
every generation. Our spin is our progenitor hole's, not our distant founder's
(Chapter~\ref{chap:nowobs}).

\section{Growth, death, and the eternal recurrence}
\label{sec:recursive-cycle}

A first universe, once viable, grows---its central holes feed and merge, its mass
climbs---and it lives a very long time: the parent black hole that hosts a
universe of our mass would take on the order of $10^{145}$ years to evaporate, far
longer than any internal cosmological clock. But it is not immortal. Its stars
burn out, its black holes themselves Hawking-evaporate, its matter redshifts away,
and the interior settles into cold ($\sim10^{-30}\,\mathrm{K}$), empty,
dark-energy-driven de Sitter space. And a vast, empty, smooth de Sitter
interior is---gravitationally---\emph{a void}. It is exactly the substrate
Chapter~\ref{chap:void} began from. So it does what a void does: it nucleates a
fresh, dilute scatter of new first universes inside itself, and the cycle turns.

\paragraph{The void is not primordial.} This collapses a hidden assumption. The
void of Chapter~\ref{chap:void} is not a separate arena that existed ``before''
everything; it is the asymptotic state every universe reaches. Universes nest both
ways---we are inside a parent, and our own far future will, in its emptiness, seed
a foam of new lineages. There is no first void and no last universe. The supraverse
is self-similar and eternal in both directions, and asks for no first cause.

\paragraph{Why heat death does not forbid it.} The obvious objection is that heat
death is \emph{maximal} entropy while a fresh bounce needs \emph{low} entropy.
Penrose's resolution applies exactly~\citep{penrose2010}. The \emph{total} entropy
rises monotonically, obeying the Second Law; but what a bounce needs small is the
\emph{gravitational} (Weyl, clumping) entropy, and that \emph{cycles}---low at the
smooth bounce, high while matter clumps into black holes, low again once the holes
evaporate and the interior smooths back to conformally flat de Sitter
(\Cref{fig:cycle}). It is the Weyl entropy a new bounce needs low, and it resets
every cycle. This is Penrose's conformal cyclic cosmology with one change: a dead
aeon here does not hand off to a single successor, it nucleates \emph{many}---a
whole foam. The framework is conformal cyclic cosmology \emph{made to branch}. (The
recurrence is a synthesis at the speculative end of the program---a natural closure
of the picture, consistent with the gravitational arrow, not a derived result.
Reproducible as \texttt{figures/scripts/ch05\_cycle.py}.)

\paragraph{And so to us.} Inside any viable universe, stellar collapse makes black
holes, and each of those is the seed of a child universe---a black-hole universe,
a BHU, the next rung down the recursion. We are on one of those rungs, not at the
top: our universe carries dark matter and dark energy, and in this framework both
\emph{require a parent} (they are the parent's mass and ongoing accretion felt
through the membrane, Chapter~\ref{chap:home}). An OGU, born straight from the
void, would have neither. So the very darkness of our sky tells us we are a child,
not a firstborn---which is the subject of the next chapter. It also rescues a
Copernican worry the framework had quietly incurred: to find ourselves a few
billion years into a Big Bang looks special, until the recurrence reveals it as
the most ordinary thing there is. Every universe has its own bounce and its own
observers a few billion years on; there have been and will be endless aeons, each a
foam of universes nested in the heat-dead past of another. We sit not at a
privileged origin but at a typical vantage---one lit pixel in an eternal,
self-similar screensaver of near-identical dawns.
