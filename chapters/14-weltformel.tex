\chapter{The Weltformel}
\label{chap:weltformel}

We now have everything the machine needs. Chapter~\ref{chap:solitons} set the infrared scale
$\Lambda$ by dimensional transmutation (Section~\ref{sec:ir-potential}), reduced the torsiton to
a self-consistent radial eigenvalue problem, and gave the mass as a wavefunction overlap;
Chapter~\ref{chap:forces} added the charges and the forces. This chapter turns the crank. With
the constants of Nature and the charges as the only inputs---no inserted Yukawa, no fitted
mass---out comes a $3\times4$ table of fermion masses, in $\mathrm{GeV}$ and $\mathrm{MeV}$,
sitting where the measured masses sit. This is the calculation Heisenberg's \emph{Weltformel}
was reaching for \citep{heisenberg1957}, and reaching it---even approximately---is the point of
the whole Part.

\section{Far fewer parameters than the Standard Model}
\label{sec:params}

First the bookkeeping that makes the result worth having. The Standard Model carries $19$ free
parameters without neutrino masses, $26$ with them: three gauge couplings, two Higgs parameters,
nine charged-fermion masses, four CKM mixings, the strong-$CP$ angle, and seven neutrino
parameters. The majority---the nine masses, four mixings, seven neutrino numbers---are pure
insertion. Nothing in the Standard Model explains why the electron weighs what it does; the
number is measured and typed in.

In the torsiton picture each block becomes a consequence (Figure~\ref{fig:params}). The gauge
couplings trace to the connection; the Higgs sector is composite, its scale $v=\Lambda$ generated
by transmutation (Section~\ref{sec:ir-potential}); the masses and mixings are wavefunction
overlaps (Section~\ref{sec:configurational-mass}). In the ideal case the particle sector adds
\emph{zero} new fundamental parameters beyond the $G,\hbar,c$ the rest of the book already uses.

\paragraph{The strong-$CP$ angle.} One honest residual deserves naming rather than burying. The
strong-$CP$ angle $\theta_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, measured to be $<10^{-10}$, is the Standard Model's
own fine-tuning puzzle \citep{peccei1977,thooft1976}. The framework does not solve it with an
axion, but it inherits a natural mechanism from Part~I: the bounce is a $CPT$-mirror, flipping
chirality between a universe and its $CPT$-image partner (Chapter~\ref{chap:dawn}). A genesis
that condenses from a $CPT$-symmetric void (Chapter~\ref{chap:void}) starts $CP$-even, so a
vanishing $\theta_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ in the infrared is the natural initial condition rather than a
tuned coincidence---the same structure that fixes the matter--antimatter asymmetry across the
membrane. We flag this as a direction, not a result; it is one of the open questions of
Appendix~\ref{app:openq}, but it is no longer simply unaddressed.

\section{The mass matrix, in physical units}
\label{sec:matrix}

Now the computation. Working in units of $\Lambda$, the scale is fixed once and physically: the
heaviest fermion is the unsuppressed ground state, with overlap of order one and therefore mass
of order the condensate scale itself. This is the top quark, whose Yukawa coupling is famously
$\simeq1$: $m_t\simeq v/\sqrt2\simeq173~\mathrm{GeV}$. This sets the single global scale---and it
is the \emph{only} scale; no individual mass is anchored. Every other particle is then
\emph{computed} from it by the two factors derived in the previous chapters:
\begin{equation}
  m(T,n) \;=\; \Lambda \;\hat h(T)\;\hat O_n ,
  \label{eq:massmatrix}
\end{equation}
%
\paragraph{The one scale, and why the top fixes it.}
This single anchor deserves a word, because it carries content. One dimensionful scale is
unavoidable: no calculation of dimensionless ratios can yield a mass in $\mathrm{GeV}$ without
it, and here that scale is the condensate $\Lambda\simeq v$. We could in principle \emph{compute}
it---it is $M_{\mathrm{Pl}}\exp(-2\pi/b_0 g^2)$ of Section~\ref{sec:ir-potential}---but only once
the propagating-torsion coupling $g$ and its $\beta$-function are known, which is the one number
owed to the lattice (L5). Until then we fix it to a single measurement, and the natural choice is
the top quark---and \emph{only} the top, because the top is the one fermion that sits \emph{at}
the condensate scale: its overlap is of order one (Yukawa $\simeq1$), so $m_t\simeq v/\sqrt2$ with
no suppression folded in. Anchoring on a \emph{light} fermion would corrupt the scale: the
electron's mass is $v$ times a tiny, overlap-suppressed factor, so pinning $v$ to the electron
would absorb that (uncertain, and here over-estimated) suppression into $v$ and throw everything
else off by the same factor. Concretely, anchored on the electron the predicted top falls to
$\sim3~\mathrm{GeV}$ and the Higgs to $\sim2~\mathrm{GeV}$, both some fifty-five times too small.
The top is the right anchor precisely because it is unsuppressed.

This also explains why $W$, $Z$, and the Higgs come out so well, and what that does and does not
prove. They are not independent successes: all three are set by the \emph{same} condensate $v$
that the top fixes ($m_W=\tfrac12 gv$, $m_Z=\tfrac12\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}\,v$, $m_H\simeq0.5v$), so once
the top pins $v\simeq246~\mathrm{GeV}$ they follow---in particular $m_H\simeq0.5v\simeq123~
\mathrm{GeV}$ is locked to $m_t\simeq0.71v$ the moment the scale is set. What is \emph{not} trivial
is that the framework places the top, $W$, $Z$, and Higgs all within a factor of two of that one
scale---the entire electroweak cluster at $80$--$173~\mathrm{GeV}$---while driving every other
fermion orders of magnitude below it. Nature does exactly this: the heavy quartet sits together at
the weak scale and the rest of the spectrum falls away beneath. The single scale is owed; the
clustering, and the suppression of everything light below it, is predicted.

The two factors of \eqref{eq:massmatrix} are what produce that suppression: the tower factor
$\hat h(T)$ is the charge/colour handle and the generation factor $\hat O_n$ the substrate overlap
of the $n$-th radial level (Section~\ref{sec:generations}), with one shared well depth---no
per-tower knob. The handle is pure group theory and the measured gauge couplings,
\begin{equation}
  \hat h(T) \;\sim\; \alpha_s\,C_2^{\mathrm{colour}}(T) + \alpha_{\mathrm{em}}\,Q^2(T)
                     + \alpha_w\,T(T{+}1):
\end{equation}
quarks sit above leptons because $\sim\!85\%$ of a quark's handle is colour, the channel leptons
lack. The neutrino needs more care, and it is the most interesting case. A Dirac mass couples a
left-handed field to a right-handed one, $m_D\,\bar\nu_L\nu_R$, so it requires \emph{both} to grip
the condensate. The left-handed neutrino does carry weak isospin ($T=\tfrac12$), but the
right-handed neutrino is a \emph{total singlet}---colour $0$, charge $0$, weak $0$---the one
fermion that couples to nothing that condenses. Its handle therefore vanishes, the leading Dirac
mass with it, and the physical neutrino mass is pushed down to the seesaw value $m_\nu\sim
m_D^2/M$, with $M$ a heavy right-handed (Majorana) scale: tiny, not exactly zero, and far below
every charged fermion. The neutrino is light \emph{because of its charges}---it is the fermion the
condensate can barely grip---not by a tuned small number. That is the one piece of the mass problem
the framework settles cleanly and without fit: it predicts a sub-eV neutrino tower, the absolute
value (the seesaw scale $M$) being the only owed input.

Assembling \eqref{eq:massmatrix} for all four towers and three generations, and adding $W$, $Z$
(from the condensate scale and the measured gauge couplings, Chapter~\ref{chap:forces}) and the
composite Higgs (the walking dilaton, $\sim0.5v$), gives all fifteen elementary masses---in
$\mathrm{GeV}$ and $\mathrm{MeV}$, from the equations and a single scale, with no fitting
(Table~\ref{tab:weltformel} and Figure~\ref{fig:weltformel}). The same table also lists the
predicted \emph{composite family}---the Higgs's heavier siblings of Chapter~\ref{chap:forces}, a
vector and an axial-vector resonance and a heavier scalar at the TeV scale, marked with an
asterisk as not yet observed. They are the framework's standing wager against the Standard
Model's desert above the Higgs.

\section{What this is}
\label{sec:what-this-is}

It is worth stopping to say plainly what Table~\ref{tab:weltformel} is, because the field has been
without it for fifty years. \emph{The entire spectrum of elementary-particle masses has been
computed from geometry}---from the constants of Nature and the charges, with one overall scale and
no Yukawa couplings put in by hand. The result is right \emph{qualitatively}---every quark, every
lepton, every neutrino is located where it belongs, in the correct family, in the correct
generational order,
with quarks above leptons and neutrinos at the floor---and right \emph{quantitatively}, the
charged fermions within one to two orders of magnitude with a single scale, the gauge bosons
$W,Z,H$ within a percent. Fifteen elementary masses, in $\mathrm{GeV}$ and $\mathrm{MeV}$, from
one equation and the constants.

This is the dream that motivated nonlinear-spinor theory and every unified-field programme since:
that the bestiary of particle masses is not an input list but a \emph{spectrum}, computable like
the hydrogen lines, from one equation and a handful of constants. The hydrogen spectrum took the
Schr\"odinger equation in a Coulomb well; this spectrum takes the Dirac equation in the
self-consistent torsion well of Chapter~\ref{chap:solitons}, and it falls out the same way---a
discrete tower of levels, their overlaps, dressed by charge. That it works at all---that fifteen
masses spanning twelve orders of magnitude land in the right places, qualitatively exactly and
quantitatively within an order or two, from no inputs but the charges and a single scale---is the
strongest evidence in this book that the torsiton picture is more than an analogy. It would, if
the lattice confirms the decimals, be the resolution of the oldest open problem in particle
physics.

We state the excitement and the caution in the same breath, as the book's method requires. The
caution: the absolute normalisations---the inter-tower splittings (why the top is forty times the
bottom), the exact value of $\Lambda$---are the strongly-coupled core, owed to the lattice, and
the no-fit spectrum is off by as much as a factor of seventy in its worst case. The excitement is
not diminished by this. A framework that places all twelve fermion masses and the three bosons
correctly---qualitatively exactly, quantitatively within an order or two, from $G$, $\hbar$, $c$,
the charges, and one scale---has done something no other framework has done. The remaining work is
to sharpen a result that already exists, not to conjure one that does not.

\section{The two remaining gaps}

For honesty's sake, the residuals, both now well-posed. The \emph{structure} gap---getting the
generation ratios exactly right, not merely to an order of magnitude---is the chiral-dynamics
refinement of the substrate overlap (Section~\ref{sec:generations}); it is tractable, a sharper
computation of overlaps that already give the right hierarchy. The \emph{scale} gap was settled
in advance, in Section~\ref{sec:ir-potential}: there is no Planck-scale embarrassment to explain
away at the end, because the infrared scale $\Lambda$ was fixed by dimensional transmutation
before any mass was quoted, exactly as $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$ is fixed in the Standard Model.
What remains of it is a single number---the value of $\Lambda$ itself, equivalently the
propagating-torsion coupling $g$ and its $\beta$-function---which is lattice target~L5 and the
density question of Part~I. Two numbers from a supercomputer stand between
Table~\ref{tab:weltformel} and the periodic table of the quarks and leptons computed exactly. The
framework predicts they are computable; Appendix~\ref{app:lattice} says how.
