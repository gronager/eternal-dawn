\chapter{The Supraverse}
\label{chap:supraverse}

\section{Global structure}

The supraverse is a single 4D manifold (3 space $+$ 1 time, Lorentzian
signature) with non-trivial topology. Most of it is essentially vacuum---
quantum-mechanically active but unstructured. Embedded in this vacuum are
condensed regions: universes, each a connected sub-manifold with its own
internal cosmological geometry, joined to the rest by Cartasis hypersurfaces.

The topology is foam-like: many universe-bubbles distributed across the
supraverse, connected to each other through nested parent--child relationships
via bounce membranes.

\section{No fifth dimension required}

The supraverse is genuinely 4D. Earlier intuitions about needing an extra
dimension to ``embed'' the parent--child relationship are visualization
crutches, not physical requirements. Mathematically, the topology is well-defined
intrinsically: two universes connected at a Cartasis hypersurface is a 4D
manifold with a particular boundary identification, no ambient space needed.

For visualization purposes, one might embed the supraverse in higher dimensions
(the Whitney embedding theorem guarantees this is possible). But that embedding
has no physical meaning---it is a drawing aid.

\section{Equilibrium configuration}

\subsection{Why condensation is favored}

Naively, one might expect universe formation to be entropically disfavored---
universes are ``ordered'' structures emerging from ``disordered'' vacuum. This
intuition is wrong because it ignores gravity. Gravitational entropy has
inverted relations compared to ordinary thermodynamics:

\begin{itemize}
  \item uniform matter distribution: low gravitational entropy;
  \item clumped matter: higher gravitational entropy;
  \item black holes: maximum entropy for given mass--energy
    (Bekenstein--Hawking, $S \propto A/4\ell_P^2$)~\citep{bekenstein1973}.
\end{itemize}

Penrose has emphasized this for decades~\citep{penrose1979}. Applied to the
supraverse: uniform vacuum has zero gravitational structure and low
gravitational entropy; a condensed foam of universes---each containing black
holes, structure, horizons---has high gravitational entropy.

\textbf{The Second Law drives the supraverse toward condensation, not away from
it.} Spontaneous universe formation is entropy-increasing when gravity is
properly accounted for. The supraverse must be in a foam state at equilibrium.

\subsection{What the equilibrium looks like}

The supraverse in its equilibrium configuration contains:

\begin{itemize}
  \item a vast quantum-vacuum background, essentially everywhere;
  \item sparse, randomly distributed universe-bubbles within this background;
  \item each universe a bubble containing its own internal cosmological structure;
  \item universes connected to each other through nested Cartasis membranes
    (parent--child relationships);
  \item a distribution of universe sizes peaking at a thermodynamically optimal
    value (see below);
  \item matter-biased and antimatter-biased lineages in equal numbers (CPT
    symmetry preserved globally).
\end{itemize}

This is the stationary distribution. Local universes are born, evolve, produce
descendants, and eventually evaporate via parent Hawking processes. New OG
universes nucleate from vacuum fluctuations. The supraverse maintains its
statistical distribution across all this dynamics.

\section{Universe size distribution}

\subsection{Birth versus growth}

Two factors compete. \emph{Births favour the small.} The probability of a vacuum
fluctuation producing Cartasis density over a region containing mass $M$ scales as
$\exp(-M c^2 \tau / \hbar)$ for some characteristic timescale $\tau$, so OGUs are
born at a small mass scale $M_0=\hbar/(c^2\tau)$ and seldom larger. \emph{Growth
favours the large.} Once born above its bath's $M_{\mathrm{crit}}$, a hole has
negative heat capacity and grows by runaway accretion at a rate
$\dot M = A\,M^{g}$---$g=1$ for Eddington-limited feeding, $g=2$ for Bondi---so
mass is continuously transported upward.

\subsection{The size distribution, derived}

In the stationary foam these two combine into a definite shape rather than a bare
conjecture. Conservation in mass space, $\partial_M[\dot M(M)\,n(M)] = B(M) -
\text{death}$, says that well above the birth scale the upward mass flux
$\dot M(M)\,n(M)$ equals the (nearly constant) total birth rate, so
\begin{equation}
  n(M) \;\propto\; \frac{1}{\dot M(M)} \;\propto\; M^{-g}
  \qquad (M_0 \lesssim M \lesssim M_{\max}),
  \label{eq:massdist}
\end{equation}
a power law---many small universes, few large---cut off at high mass $M_{\max}$ by
environment depletion and the stationary balance (\Cref{fig:ogudist}, left). Since
$n(M)$ falls with $M$, the typical universe is as small as viability allows: a
low-mass cut $M_{\mathrm{vis}}$ (enough mass for astrophysics and black-hole
formation) selects observer-bearing universes, which therefore pile up just above
$M_{\mathrm{vis}}$. This is the old ``we sit near the optimum'' conjecture, now
\emph{derived}: we expect to find ourselves near the viability edge of a declining
power law, not out in the exponentially rare massive tail.

\section{Lineages and the family tree}

Each OG universe seeds a lineage tree of descendants. The tree branches at each
black-hole formation within a universe (each black hole potentially produces a
baby universe).

\subsection{Branching structure}

A typical viable universe contains many stellar-mass black holes ($\sim 10^{18}$
in a galaxy-rich universe like ours), some supermassive black holes
($\sim 10^{9}$ across all galaxies), and possibly primordial black holes from
early-universe dynamics. Each is a potential baby universe, so a typical lineage
tree branches widely at each generation. The tree depth is limited by the finite
Hawking lifetime of each generation, by the requirement that each generation be
viable enough to produce its own black holes, and by the cumulative effects of
flip $+$ filter on chirality.

\subsection{The generation-depth distribution}
\label{sec:gendepth}

The branching above invites a sharper question than ``how wide is the tree?'',
namely \emph{how deep are we}---is our universe an original-generation universe
(OGU, which we label $\mathrm{BHU}_0$), or $\mathrm{BHU}_1$, $\mathrm{BHU}_2$,
$\ldots$? This is the birth-versus-growth balance made quantitative, and it was
previously only asserted; we now derive its structure as a bounded branching
process. Let $N$ be the mean number of \emph{viable} children per universe---not
the $\sim10^{18}$ holes, but the far smaller count that both exceed their
internal-bath $M_{\mathrm{crit}}$ and clear the chiral-vortical birth filter
$|\omega/T|>\eta_{\min}/C$ (Section~\ref{sec:genesis-void})---and let a fraction
$p$ of children remain fertility-preserving, so the effective per-generation
reproduction is $m=Np$. Counting universes, the number at depth $n$ scales as
$m^n$ up to a viability truncation, giving
\begin{equation}
  P(n) \;=\; \frac{m^{\,n}}{\sum_{k=0}^{D} m^{\,k}}, \qquad n = 0,1,\dots,D.
  \label{eq:gendepth}
\end{equation}
The regime is everything (\Cref{fig:gendepth}, left). If $m<1$ the lineage is
subcritical and dies out, so most universes are shallow; if $m>1$ it is
supercritical and the population piles up at the deepest viable generation, so a
typical universe is \emph{deep}; $m=1$ is scale-free. Which regime the supraverse
is in is set by Einstein--Cartan microphysics through $N$ and the viability decay
$p$, and is not yet pinned down.

\subsection{We are not an OGU}
\label{sec:notogu}

One datum settles the qualitative answer independently of the branching
parameters. Our universe contains dark matter and dark energy, and in this
framework both \emph{require a parent}: dark matter is parent material projected
through the persistent membrane, and dark energy is the parent's ongoing
accretion (Chapter~\ref{chap:darksector}). An OGU is nucleated directly from the
void with no parent (Section~\ref{sec:genesis-void}); it would have neither
component. Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \text{(observed dark matter and dark energy)} \;\Longrightarrow\;
  n \ge 1 :
  \text{ we are not a $\mathrm{BHU}_0$.}
\end{equation}
This is robust. The branching prior \eqref{eq:gendepth} then determines whether,
given $n\ge1$, we are shallow ($\mathrm{BHU}_1$, $\mathrm{BHU}_2$) or deep: if the
foam is supercritical, a typical observer is many generations down
(\Cref{fig:gendepth}, right). The depth is presently a range, not a number; it
will tighten as the dark-matter fraction $\Omega_{\mathrm{dm}}/\Omega_b$, the
dark-energy history $w(a)$, and the asymmetry coupling $C$ are turned into
quantitative constraints on the parent. We expect the visualization
(Chapter~\ref{chap:viz}) to be revised downward or upward in depth as that
happens.

\subsection{Lineage chirality structure}
\label{sec:lineage-chirality}

The matter--antimatter bias is selected \emph{once, at the OG seed}, and
\emph{inherited} down the lineage---it is not re-rolled at each bounce. This is
the load-bearing mechanism of Chapter~\ref{chap:chirality}: the Einstein--Cartan
bounce is CPT-even and is an \emph{amplifier, not a source}, so a bounce with no
net axial input gives no net output. The one place an asymmetry can originate is
the OG nucleation, where the seed fluctuation's net vorticity $\omega$ freezes in
a bias $|\eta|\sim C\,|\omega/T|$ whose \emph{sign} fixes whether that lineage is
matter- or antimatter-led (Section~\ref{sec:genesis-void}). Because the vacuum is
CPT-symmetric the seed-sign distribution is even: matter- and antimatter-led OG
lineages occur in equal numbers and global CPT is preserved.

Thereafter the bias is passed down, not reset. A matter-biased parent feeds
matter through the membrane, so its black holes are matter black holes and its
descendant universes are matter universes; an antimatter parent yields antimatter
descendants. \emph{Lineages are chirally pure}---a matter OGU contains only matter
$\mathrm{BHU}_n$, an antimatter OGU only antimatter, with no mixing---and the
mean-field amplification at each subsequent bounce can sharpen the inherited
purity but never flip its sign. Baryonic selection happens at the top, to a
purity of order $10^{-8}$ (set by the seed vorticity), and is inherited from
there.

\textbf{Observer placement:} observers exist in lineages whose \emph{OG seed}
cleared the viability threshold---enough seed vorticity to leave matter after
annihilation and form structure, $|\omega/T| > \eta_{\min}/C$. This is a one-step
\emph{birth filter on the OG seed} (Section~\ref{sec:spin-dist}), inherited by all
descendants, not a condition re-imposed at every crossing.

\subsection{What a hole eats: two families, and our parentage}
\label{sec:accretion-eta}

The sign of $\eta$ is strictly inherited, but its \emph{magnitude} is not a clean
lineage invariant: it is set by \emph{what the parent hole ate}. Because the
bounce is adiabatic ($D=1$; \Cref{sec:extruder}), entropy is conserved across the
crossing, so the child does not regenerate its photons---it inherits the
baryon-to-photon ratio of the accreted material itself:
\begin{equation}
  \eta_{\mathrm{child}} = \eta_{\mathrm{infall}} = b\,\eta_{\mathrm{parent}},
  \qquad b \equiv \frac{\eta_{\mathrm{infall}}}{\eta_{\mathrm{cosmic}}},
  \label{eq:eta-accretion}
\end{equation}
capped at $\eta\sim1$. Here $b$ is the baryon bias of the infall: $b=1$ for a fair
cosmic sample (a horizon-scale hole, or a radiation-era primordial hole, which
contain the cosmic ratio by construction); $b>1$ for concentrated-baryon
accretion (stars and gas have few thermal photons per baryon), driving the child
toward a baryon-rich, degenerate $\eta\to1$; $b<1$ for photon-biased accretion
(void or intergalactic medium), giving a cleaner child. So a growing hole really
does carry ``a different $\eta$ inside than how the parent was born,'' and the
\emph{direction} of that drift is signed by the accretion bias.

This splits each chiral lineage into \emph{two families}
(\Cref{fig:accretion_depth}, left): a clean, photon-dominated family
($\eta\ll1$, fed by fair or radiation-dominated capture) and a baryon-rich,
degenerate family ($\eta\to1$, fed by stars and dense gas). Our universe is
firmly in the clean family ($\eta\simeq6\times10^{-10}\simeq\eta_{\mathrm{cosmic}}$,
i.e.\ $b\sim1$). That is a structural \emph{prediction about our parentage}: our
progenitor hole accreted a fair cosmic sample---a horizon-scale or radiation-fed
hole---not concentrated baryons. A star-fed hole ($b\sim10^{9}$) would have
spawned a degenerate, baryon-saturated universe utterly unlike ours.

\subsection{Are we likely BHU1 or BHU2?}
\label{sec:bhu-depth}

The robust anchor (\Cref{sec:notogu}) fixes $n\ge1$. How much deeper? Counting
universes, generation $n$ holds $N_n\sim m^n$ of them, so
$P(\mathrm{BHU}_n\mid n\ge1)\propto m^n$, where $m$ is the mean number of
\emph{viable} children per universe. The answer is a clean dichotomy
(\Cref{fig:accretion_depth}, right): for a \emph{subcritical} branching $m<1$ the
distribution is geometric, $P(\mathrm{BHU}_n)=(1-m)m^{n-1}$, the population is
shallow, and we are most likely $\mathrm{BHU}_1$; for $m>1$ the population is
dominated by the deepest generation the supraverse has had time to grow, and being
$\mathrm{BHU}_{1\text{--}2}$ is exponentially unlikely. So everything turns on
whether $m$ is below or above one.

It is tempting to put $m$ enormous---a universe forms $\gtrsim10^{18}$ black holes,
almost all super-critical---and conclude we are surely deep. But that
\emph{double-counts}: a stellar hole ($\sim10\,M_\odot$) makes a $\sim10^{31}\,
\mathrm{kg}$ child, a tiny, structureless universe far below the viability mass
$M_{\mathrm{vis}}$. \emph{Viable} children need near-Hubble-mass progenitor holes,
and mass conservation allows only $\sim M_{\mathrm{parent}}/M_{\mathrm{vis}}$ of
those: a universe simply has not got the mass to make $10^{18}$ universe-sized
children. The branching ratio is therefore
\begin{equation}
  m = \epsilon\,f_{\mathrm{clean}}\,\frac{M_{\mathrm{parent}}}{M_{\mathrm{vis}}},
  \label{eq:m-massbudget}
\end{equation}
with $\epsilon$ the fraction of the parent's mass reaching viable-size holes and
$f_{\mathrm{clean}}$ the fraction that accrete a fair ($b\sim1$) sample and so stay
in our clean family. The viable band is \emph{narrow on both axes at once}: the big
horizon-scale holes that clear the size cut are the very ones that contain a fair
cosmic sample (\Cref{sec:accretion-eta}), so size and cleanliness select the same
rare holes. If the band is narrow---$M_{\mathrm{vis}}\sim$ our mass, the edge we
appear to sit at, so $M_{\mathrm{parent}}/M_{\mathrm{vis}}\sim O(1)$---then
$m\sim\epsilon<1$: subcritical, shallow, and we are most likely $\mathrm{BHU}_1$
(\Cref{fig:viable_band}). Only a \emph{wide} band ($M_{\mathrm{vis}}\ll
M_{\mathrm{parent}}$) gives $m\gg1$ and a deep population. Narrowness, not a young
supraverse, is the natural route to shallowness---and it follows from mass
conservation plus the single viability scale we already seem to sit near. Only
$n\ge1$ is settled by data, but a narrow viable band makes
$\mathrm{BHU}_{1\text{--}2}$ the expectation rather than a surprise.

\subsection{Why BHU1 is robust---and how big the OGU is}
\label{sec:ogu-size}

The branching ratio is not the same at every generation, and that is what makes
the conclusion robust. An OGU has \emph{grown} large by runaway accretion before it
spawns anything, so $M_{\mathrm{OGU}}\gg M_{\mathrm{vis}}$ and the OGU is
\emph{super}-critical: it spawns $m_0=\epsilon\,M_{\mathrm{OGU}}/M_{\mathrm{vis}}
\gg1$ first-generation children. But those children are born \emph{at} the
viability scale ($M\sim M_{\mathrm{vis}}$---the edge we observe ourselves to
occupy), so each is \emph{sub}-critical, $m_{n\ge1}=\epsilon\,M/M_{\mathrm{vis}}
\sim\epsilon<1$. The population is therefore one OGU $\to$ many BHU1 $\to$ a
geometrically decaying tail, $N_n\sim m_0\,\epsilon^{\,n-1}$, and the huge $m_0$
\emph{cancels} in the $n\ge1$ normalisation:
\begin{equation}
  P(\mathrm{BHU}_n\mid n\ge1) = (1-\epsilon)\,\epsilon^{\,n-1},
  \label{eq:gen-depth}
\end{equation}
so $P(\mathrm{BHU}_1)=1-\epsilon\approx0.9$ for $\epsilon\sim0.1$, \emph{whatever}
the OGU's size. We are BHU1 because our generation sits at $M_{\mathrm{vis}}$ and
barely reproduces, not because the supraverse is small or young.

This also answers ``how big is an OGU?''---and shows the answer is decoupled from
our depth. The number of BHU1 siblings an OGU produces is
$N_{\mathrm{sib}}\sim\epsilon\,M_{\mathrm{OGU}}/M_{\mathrm{vis}}$, so
\begin{equation}
  M_{\mathrm{OGU}}\sim \frac{N_{\mathrm{sib}}}{\epsilon}\,M_{\mathrm{vis}}.
\end{equation}
With $M_{\mathrm{vis}}\sim$ our mass $\sim9\times10^{52}\,\mathrm{kg}$ and
$\epsilon\sim0.1$: a single-child OGU is only $\sim10^{54}\,\mathrm{kg}$, while a
$\sim10^{65}\,\mathrm{kg}$ OGU has $\sim10^{11}$ sibling universes like ours. Both
are equally compatible with our being BHU1. The actual figure is set by how much
void the OGU entrains and depletes before freezing
(\Cref{sec:growth-depth})---the genesis/vortex problem of the next section and
of \Cref{app:openq}---so it is genuinely open: anywhere from a few times our mass
up to many orders above it. What the framework fixes is not the OGU mass but our
\emph{place}: a BHU1, one of an OGU's possibly enormous brood.

\subsection{Growth, depletion, and the depth dial}
\label{sec:growth-depth}

What sets the branching depth is how fast a hole grows into a universe and spawns
the next generation. The dynamics are nearly parameter-free. A hole of mass $M$ in
a void bath at temperature $T_{\mathrm{bath}}$ emits Hawking radiation at $T_H$ and
absorbs the bath through the same area, so the net rate is the Hawking power times
the temperature contrast,
\begin{equation}
  \dot M = \frac{P_H}{c^2}\!\left[\Big(\frac{M}{M_{\mathrm{crit}}}\Big)^{4}-1\right],
  \qquad M_{\mathrm{crit}}=\frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G k_B T_{\mathrm{bath}}},
  \label{eq:growth}
\end{equation}
so below $M_{\mathrm{crit}}$ the hole evaporates and above it the bath wins and it
runs away as $\dot M\sim M^2$---the Bondi exponent $g=2$ behind the
$n(M)\sim M^{-2}$ mass law of \Cref{sec:notogu}. In units $x=M/M_{\mathrm{crit}}$,
$t'=t/\tau_0$ with $\tau_0=3\,t_{\mathrm{Hawking}}(M_{\mathrm{crit}})$, the law is
universal, $\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t'=(x^4-1)/x^2$. The runaway is \emph{fast}---a
finite-time blow-up at $t'\!\sim\!1/x_0$---but it cannot actually blow up, because
\emph{causality} caps it: a hole cannot accrete matter outside its own causal
horizon, so $\dot M$ saturates at the rate at which the horizon mass itself grows,
\begin{equation}
  \dot M \;\lesssim\; \frac{c^3}{2G} \;\approx\; 2\times10^{35}\,\mathrm{kg\,s^{-1}}
  \;\sim\; 3\times10^{12}\,M_\odot\,\mathrm{yr^{-1}}.
  \label{eq:causal-rate}
\end{equation}
The runaway therefore self-limits the moment its accretion radius reaches the
horizon, and thereafter the hole grows \emph{steadily}---it extracts ``nothing''
from the void at a fixed rate---so $M\sim c^3t/2G$, the horizon-mass growth law of
\Cref{sec:vortex-genesis}. Whether it ever stops depends on the environment, and
this is where the two regulators part. An OGU sits in the \emph{infinite} void, so
fresh vacuum perpetually crosses into its growing horizon: it never runs out, and
grows as $c^3t/2G$ for as long as the void lasts. A hole inside a \emph{finite}
parent is different: its parent's dark-energy expansion carries matter beyond
reach faster than it can eat, so it \emph{depletes} its accessible patch and
freezes at $M_{\mathrm{final}}\sim$ the patch mass (\Cref{fig:growth}, left). That
is the faintly comic fate of an internal universe-hole---fed by the void, it dies
in the end of depletion: it runs out of nothing. So the two pictures are one law
read in two settings: \emph{rate-limited and unbounded} in the infinite void
(the OGUs), \emph{depletion-limited and finite} inside a parent (their descendants).
This answers the runaway worry of \Cref{app:openq}: no hole blows up; the OGUs grow
steadily with the horizon, their descendants freeze at their patch.

The growth time is therefore $\tau_{\mathrm{gen}}\sim\tau_0\propto
M_{\mathrm{crit}}^3\propto T_{\mathrm{bath}}^{-3}$, and a supraverse of age
$T_{\mathrm{supra}}$ has grown at most $D_{\max}=T_{\mathrm{supra}}/
\tau_{\mathrm{gen}}$ generations. This is a hard cap on our depth, set entirely by
the void bath temperature (\Cref{fig:growth}, right). It closes the population loop
of \Cref{sec:bhu-depth}: if the void is too cold the foam is sterile (no BHU forms
in the available time)---\emph{excluded, because we are a BHU}, which already puts
a lower bound on $T_{\mathrm{bath}}$; a Goldilocks $T_{\mathrm{bath}}\sim10^{12}\,
\mathrm{K}$ (for a Hubble-aged supraverse) gives $D_{\max}\sim1$, forcing us to be
$\mathrm{BHU}_1$ and matching the shallow reading; a hotter void or an older
supraverse gives $D_{\max}\gg1$ and the depth reverts to the branching ratio $m$
(deep). The honest upshot: ``are we $\mathrm{BHU}_{1\text{--}2}$?'' is now a sharp
function of two unknowns---the void temperature and the supraverse age (\Cref{app:openq})---
rather than a guess, and our existence as $n\ge1$ already excludes the cold,
sterile corner. Reproducible as \texttt{figures/scripts/ch05\_growth.py}.

\section{OG bounce nucleation rate}

The rate of OG bounce nucleation depends on the vacuum-fluctuation spectrum at
high energies (set by QFT), the Cartasis density threshold (set by
Einstein--Cartan parameters), and the available supraverse spacetime volume.

The recursive cycle (\Cref{sec:recursive-cycle}) lets us say more than ``calculable
in principle.'' Since OGUs nucleate in the heat-dead de Sitter void of an older
universe, $\beta$ is a \emph{de Sitter nucleation rate}, and its scale is set---not
free---by the void's dark energy. Writing it per de Sitter horizon four-volume,
\begin{equation}
  \beta = \lambda\,\frac{H_\Lambda^4}{c^3},\qquad
  H_\Lambda = H_0\sqrt{\Omega_\Lambda}\simeq1.8\times10^{-18}\,\mathrm{s^{-1}},
  \label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
the prefactor is fixed by the observed dark energy (by typicality the void's
$\Lambda$ is $\sim$ours): $H_\Lambda^4/c^3\simeq4\times10^{-97}\,\mathrm{m^{-3}
s^{-1}}$, one nucleation ``attempt'' per Hubble four-volume. The one remaining
unknown is the dimensionless $\lambda=e^{-I}$, with $I$ the action of the
$\rhoc$-crossing gravitational instanton (quantum gravity at the bounce,
\Cref{app:openq})---but it carries a \emph{sharp critical value}. De Sitter bubble
percolation~\citep{guth1983} sets $\lambda_{\mathrm{crit}}\simeq0.24$, an instanton
action $I_{\mathrm{crit}}=-\ln\lambda_{\mathrm{crit}}\simeq1.4$, and that one number
fixes the \emph{character} of the supraverse (\Cref{fig:birthrate}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item $I<1.4$ (a near-Planckian seed): OGUs nucleate faster than de Sitter
    dilutes, \emph{percolate}, and tile the void into the packed polygonal foam that
    coarsens (\Cref{sec:foam-size,sec:foam-dynamics}).
  \item $I>1.4$ (a macroscopic seed): the void inflates between nucleations, so OGUs
    stay isolated, round, and never impinge---a \emph{dilute} scatter of island
    universes, the eternal-inflation regime, with no coarsening.
\end{itemize}
Either way we are a $\mathrm{BHU}_1$ inside an OGU---the internal black-hole channel
is independent of how OGUs are spaced. So $\beta$ is pinned in scale (the dark-energy
prefactor) and in its decisive structure ($I_{\mathrm{crit}}\simeq1.4$); only the
bounce instanton $I$, and with it the packed-versus-dilute regime, remains open.

\subsection{Computing the instanton action---and the verdict}
\label{sec:instanton}

The action $I$ is not merely ``calculable in principle''; the de Sitter framework
hands us a closed form. OG nucleation is an \emph{upward} fluctuation in the void: a
patch of cold vacuum ($\rho_\Lambda\sim10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$) must spike to
$\rhoc\sim10^{50}\,\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$ and bounce. In de Sitter at temperature
$T_{\mathrm{dS}}=\hbar H_\Lambda/2\pi k_B$ the rate of creating a mass $M$ is the
Boltzmann factor $e^{-Mc^2/k_BT_{\mathrm{dS}}}$, and that exponent \emph{equals} the
cosmological-horizon entropy deficit of inserting $M$ (the rigorous de Sitter
identity $2\pi Mc^2/\hbar H = \pi r_{\mathrm{dS}} r_s/\ell_p^2$). So
\begin{equation}
  I = \frac{2\pi M_{\mathrm{seed}}\,c^2}{\hbar H_\Lambda},
  \label{eq:instanton}
\end{equation}
and the only input is the lightest seed that can actually bounce. A blob at $\rhoc$
bounces as a universe only if it is self-gravitating, $R_s\simeq R$, which fixes
$R\sim c/\Omega_{\mathrm{bounce}}$ and
$M_{\mathrm{seed}}=c^3/(2G\Omega_{\mathrm{bounce}})\sim9\times10^{14}\,\mathrm{kg}$
(a lighter blob has $R_s\ll R$, no horizon, and disperses).

Plugging in gives the answer (\Cref{fig:instanton}):
\begin{equation}
  I \;\sim\; 2.5\times10^{84} \;\gg\; I_{\mathrm{crit}}\simeq1.4 .
\end{equation}
The margin is eighty-four orders of magnitude---no semiclassical correction, no
factor-of-ten ambiguity in the seed, can close it. To reach $I_{\mathrm{crit}}$ one
would need a seed of $\sim5\times10^{-70}\,\mathrm{kg}$ (sub-Planckian by sixty
orders, unable to bounce) or a void at $\sim10^{54}\,\mathrm{K}$ (the actual void is
at $10^{-30}\,\mathrm{K}$). \textbf{So $\lambda=e^{-I}$ is, for all purposes, zero,
and the supraverse is firmly in the \emph{dilute} regime}: OGUs are isolated, round,
eternal-inflation-like island universes, astronomically far apart---\emph{not} a
percolating polygonal foam.

\paragraph{What this revises, and what it leaves standing.} Honesty demands the
admission: the packed-foam constructions---the average OGU size by impingement
(\Cref{sec:foam-size}), the radiative coarsening (\Cref{sec:foam-dynamics}), the
polygonal render of \Cref{chap:viz}---are the \emph{percolating limit}, and the
computation says that limit is not realised. The physical supraverse is dilute. What
survives untouched is everything that does not depend on OGU spacing: we are a
$\mathrm{BHU}_1$ inside an OGU (the internal black-hole channel is independent of how
OGUs are scattered); the recursive cycle still closes (each isolated island
heat-dies and seeds its own dilute scatter, \Cref{sec:recursive-cycle}); the
horizon-mass growth $M_{\mathrm{OGU}}\sim c^3t/2G$ and the dark-energy-pinned
prefactor stand. And the dilute verdict \emph{vindicates} an earlier reading: a
well-separated universe is round, not polygonal (\Cref{sec:foam-size})---which is
exactly what isolated island universes are. The framework thus lands, by
computation, in the eternal-inflation family: a sparse, ever-inflating scatter of
recursive island universes, each a Cartasis bounce.

\subsection{Settlers, not city dwellers: growth and the weight of the void}
\label{sec:dilute-consequences}

Two features of the foam picture must be re-read in the dilute regime.

\paragraph{Does growth still run out of nothing?} Yes, but for a new reason. In the
packed limit, growth slowed because neighbours ate the shared void (impingement). In
the dilute regime there are no neighbours within reach---OGUs are settlers, not city
dwellers---so impingement never happens. Instead a lone OGU's growth is choked by the
de Sitter \emph{expansion} of its own void: it can only gather what lies inside the
cosmological horizon $R_{\mathrm{dS}}=c/H_\Lambda$, and the accelerating expansion
carries everything else beyond reach. The rigorous statement is the Nariai bound---
the largest black hole that fits in de Sitter has
$M_{\mathrm{Nariai}}=c^3/(3\sqrt3\,GH_\Lambda)\simeq4\times10^{52}\,\mathrm{kg}$---so
the OGU mass is \emph{capped at $\sim$a Hubble mass, our own universe's mass, set by
dark energy}. So a settler still runs out of nothing: not because a neighbour took
it, but because the frontier itself recedes faster than it can be settled. (This caps
the age-clock growth $M\sim c^3t/2G$ of \Cref{sec:vortex-genesis} at the Nariai mass;
the earlier unbounded values were the static/percolating idealisation.)

\paragraph{How much space does the void take?} The vacuum is not weightless. Its
zero-point / dark-energy density is
$\rho_\Lambda=\Omega_\Lambda\,\tfrac{3H_0^2}{8\pi G}\simeq6\times10^{-27}\,
\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$ ($\sim3\,\mathrm{GeV\,m^{-3}}$, a few proton masses per cubic
metre). Since $E=\rho c^2$ gives it weight, it occupies \emph{nonzero} gravity-scaled
volume---so universes do not touch, and the gravity-scaled map shows \emph{round},
isolated islands with real gaps, not polygons. And because the regime is dilute
(separations $\sim e^{I/4}$ horizons), that weighted void overwhelmingly dominates:
true to scale, the supraverse is almost entirely void, with vanishingly sparse round
island universes (\Cref{chap:viz}, \Cref{fig:dilute}). The void's weight is exactly
what gives the islands room to be round; the dilution makes that room enormous. So
the honest ``true to scale'' wallpaper is mostly void---which is, after all, what an
elegant theory of an eternal structured vacuum should look like.

\subsection{An OGU is a timeline: saturation, evaporation, and a mortal fractal}
\label{sec:recursion-depth}

An OGU is not merely a universe; it is a \emph{timeline}---it grows, hosts an
internal history, and eventually ends. Three questions close the recursive picture:
does it grow forever, does the membrane ever disappear, and is the nesting literally
infinite?

\paragraph{Saturation is geometric, not thermal.} Growth stops---but \emph{not}, as a
careless reading suggests, because the OGU reaches thermal equilibrium with the void.
That reading creates a paradox (if it is in equilibrium, why does it evaporate?), and
the resolution is the proper two-horizon treatment. Schwarzschild--de Sitter has
\emph{two} horizons---the black-hole horizon $r_b$ and the cosmological horizon
$r_c>r_b$, with the universe living \emph{between} them (\Cref{fig:sds}, left). As the
mass grows the two approach and finally merge at the Nariai mass,
\begin{equation}
  M_{\mathrm{Nariai}} = \frac{c^3}{3\sqrt3\,GH_\Lambda} \simeq 4\times10^{52}\,
  \mathrm{kg} \;\sim\; \text{a Hubble mass} \;\sim\; \text{our universe},
  \label{eq:Meq}
\end{equation}
the largest black hole that geometrically \emph{fits} in this de Sitter space. So the
cap is a geometric maximum---no bigger horizon exists---reached in $\sim$1 Hubble
time, not a thermal balance. Dark energy starts the growth (negative heat capacity)
and bounds it (the horizon can grow no larger).

\paragraph{Why it nonetheless evaporates.} The two horizons have different
temperatures, $T_b=|f'(r_b)|/4\pi$ and $T_c=|f'(r_c)|/4\pi$, and the decisive fact is
that \emph{$T_b>T_c$ for every sub-Nariai mass}, with equality only at the Nariai
point itself (\Cref{fig:sds}, right). The black hole is \emph{always} hotter than its
own cosmological horizon, so net radiation always flows outward and the OGU
\emph{always} evaporates---there is no true equilibrium except the unstable,
measure-zero Nariai coincidence. What looked like ``equilibrium, growth stops'' is
really a near-Nariai \emph{lukewarm} plateau where $T_b-T_c\to0$ and evaporation
becomes arbitrarily slow, but never zero. So the OGU caps at $\sim M_{\mathrm{Nariai}}$
(geometry) and then drains on the Hawking time $t_{\mathrm{evap}}\sim5120\pi G^2
M^3/\hbar c^4\sim10^{142}\,\mathrm{s}$ (the residual $T_b>T_c$)---finite, vast, and
long before it could ever touch a neighbour ($\sim e^{I/4}$ horizons away,
\Cref{sec:instanton}). Your guess was right: it Hawking-evaporates before touching
anything; the cap and the evaporation are simply geometry and thermodynamics, not a
contradiction.

\paragraph{Does time inside stop when the membrane goes?} No---and ``no
discontinuities'' (the organizing principle, Chapter~\ref{chap:axioms}) is exactly
the guide. The membrane is
the baby's \emph{past} cosmological horizon (its Big-Bang surface), not its future;
the baby's own time runs forward to its \emph{own} de Sitter infinity, eternally
(\Cref{sec:recursion-depth} below). Exterior evaporation and interior eternity are
\emph{duals} across the null horizon, not a clash: an infinite interior proper time
maps to the finite exterior horizon lifetime. By the (exterior) time the parent's
horizon has evaporated, the baby has already---on its own clock---expanded to its de
Sitter future, i.e.\ \emph{become} a flat, heat-dead void hosting its own scatter of
sub-OGUs. So the parent's evaporation is just the exterior face of the interior
reaching its natural conformal infinity: the umbilical channel pinches off (the
connection ends), but no clock is stopped and no nested structure is destroyed. The
inner universes were always inside the baby's own far-future void, which simply
becomes a free-standing region of the eternal flat substrate, carrying its
sub-structure with it. Nothing inside an evaporating membrane ends; it is merely cut
loose into the void it was always becoming.

\section{Genesis, growth, and the absence of hell}

\subsection{No hell upward}

An older worry held that, climbing the lineage toward earlier ancestors, a
compounding filter would make ancestors progressively more symmetric---less
asymmetry, more annihilation, until near-symmetric, sterile universes that cannot
form black holes (the ``hell upward'' problem). With the CPT-even bounce this
dissolves. There is no charge flip and so no alternating chirality to climb
(Chapter~\ref{chap:chirality}); and because the bias is \emph{inherited from a
single OG seed} rather than re-derived at each crossing
(Section~\ref{sec:lineage-chirality}), climbing the lineage does not dilute it.
Every ancestor of a matter universe is itself a matter universe of the same
inherited purity (possibly sharpened by amplification, never weakened). The upward
end of the chain is not a tower of ever-more-symmetric ancestors---it is the OG
seed and, beyond it, the void (Section~\ref{sec:genesis-void}). The whole lineage
is viable because its single founding seed cleared the spin threshold; viability
is decided once, at the top, not re-won at every generation.

\subsection{The void and OG genesis}
\label{sec:genesis-void}

An OG universe is nucleated directly from the primordial void, with no parent. It
cannot inherit a bias, so its asymmetry must be \emph{made} at the OG bounce
itself---and the rotating-bounce chiral-vortical filter does exactly that,
writing matter at the very first crossing. The void is the
low-gravitational-entropy, CPT-symmetric substrate; genesis is the Second Law
acting on it, condensing rotating fluctuations into bounces. Most fluctuations
are too small or too slowly rotating to clear the birth filter and produce only
sterile radiation; the rare viable ones seed everything. \emph{Most supraverse
structure is concentrated in the successful OG lineages}; failed attempts do not
propagate.

\subsection{Spin: the viability filter and our purity}
\label{sec:spin-dist}

The phrase ``too slowly rotating'' is the second derivable distribution, and it is
sharper than the size one. The seed's net vorticity $\omega$ sets the inherited
asymmetry $|\eta|\sim C|\omega/T|$ (Section~\ref{sec:lineage-chirality}). A random
fluctuation most likely has \emph{small} net vorticity---the birth distribution
$P_{\mathrm{birth}}(\omega)$ is peaked at $\omega=0$, its sign even (whence equal
matter/antimatter lineages). But a seed with $|\eta|<\eta_{\min}$ cannot complete
baryogenesis: matter and antimatter annihilate to near-symmetric radiation, no
structure or black holes form, and the universe is sterile---it simply evaporates
and seeds nothing. Viability therefore demands $|\omega| > \omega_{\min} =
\eta_{\min}T/C$. The result is a competition exactly opposite to intuition:
\emph{no spin is the most likely outcome but the most hellish}, while \emph{high
spin is rare but pure}. Folding the viability gate (and the mild productivity bonus
of higher purity) against the birth distribution,
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{obs}}(\omega) \;\propto\; P_{\mathrm{birth}}(\omega)\,
  \bigl[\text{productivity}(\omega)\bigr]\,\mathbf{1}\!\left[|\omega|>\omega_{\min}\right],
  \label{eq:spindist}
\end{equation}
gives a distribution peaked \emph{just above} the threshold
(\Cref{fig:ogudist}, right): the typical viable universe is \emph{low}-spin and
\emph{low}-purity, only marginally past sterile. This is a \emph{shape} prediction,
and it carries a striking qualitative payoff: it explains \emph{why the baryon
asymmetry is small without fine-tuning}. The observed $\eta\sim10^{-9}$--$10^{-8}$
is exactly what one expects if our universe is a typical viable one---barely past
the sterility threshold---rather than a rare high-purity outlier. We must be
careful about how strong a claim this is. The peak in \Cref{fig:ogudist} is plotted
in units of the (unknown) birth-vorticity scale $\sigma$ with the threshold
$\omega_{\min}/\sigma$ chosen by hand; the model has \emph{not} predicted
$\eta\approx6\times10^{-10}$ in absolute units, and the figure marker is the
model's own peak, not an independent measurement of our spin. A genuinely spot-on
test requires pinning $C$, $\sigma$, and $\eta_{\min}$ from Einstein--Cartan
microphysics so that $\eta$ is predicted as a number. What is robust now is the
\emph{shape}---most viable universes are barely viable---and the qualitative
consequence that a small cosmic asymmetry is the expected, typical case. The model
also predicts a small but \emph{nonzero} net rotation for our universe, to be
confronted with reported galaxy-spin handedness asymmetries (\citealp{shamir2025};
Chapter~\ref{chap:tests}) and large-angle CMB axis alignments.

\subsection{Growth: biased runaway accretion}

The condensation favoured by the Second Law has a concrete microphysics: black
holes have negative heat capacity, so above a critical mass
$M_{\mathrm{crit}} = \hbar c^3/(8\pi G k_B T_{\mathrm{bath}})$ a hole accretes
faster than it Hawking-radiates and grows. (In today's CMB bath
$M_{\mathrm{crit}}\sim 5\times10^{22}\,\mathrm{kg}$, so every astrophysical black
hole is already growing.) Because the Cartasis interface is persistent
(Chapter~\ref{chap:baby}), the parity-violating filter keeps acting throughout
the parent's life, so the growth is \emph{biased}---the hole preferentially
retains one charge sign of what it accretes. Two consequences: baryogenesis is
\emph{ongoing} rather than one-shot (helping the single-bounce shortfall of
Chapter~\ref{chap:chirality}), and the migration of the void's free energy into
the growing universe-holes \emph{is} the condensation that makes the foam its
equilibrium. From inside a baby universe this continuous biased injection is the
dark sector (Chapter~\ref{chap:darksector}).

\subsection{Vortex entrainment and the OGU mass}
\label{sec:vortex-genesis}

What \emph{drives} that growth from a bare seed, and what fixes how big an OGU
gets? The rotation does both. A spinning bounce drags inertial frames
(Lense--Thirring, $\Omega_{\mathrm{drag}}\sim 2GJ/c^2r^3$): the throat is a
gravitational \emph{vortex} that spins up and draws in the surrounding void---the
rotational analogue of Bondi capture, and the concrete meaning of the seed
``pumping'' primordial matter from the void into the new universe. The pump has a
\emph{spin window} (\Cref{fig:genesis}, right). Below
$f_{\min}=\eta_{\min}/[C\,(\Omega_{\mathrm{bounce}}/T)]$ (the spin fraction
$f=\omega/\Omega_{\mathrm{bounce}}$) the chiral-vortical asymmetry is too small to
survive annihilation---a sterile seed that pumps nothing. Near extremal ($f\to1$)
the centrifugal barrier chokes radial inflow: matter circularises instead of
falling in. So entrainment is most efficient at an intermediate \emph{sweet spot},
which is precisely the productivity factor of the observed-spin distribution
(\Cref{sec:spin-dist}).

The entrained mass is then set by a horizon argument, not by bookkeeping the
inflow. The vortex reaches the void within its causal radius $R\sim ct$, and a
patch of radius $ct$ holds at most the mass whose Schwarzschild radius equals
$ct$, $M_{\mathrm{horizon}}=c^3t/2G$. If the void is at least critically dense
($\rho_{\mathrm{void}}\ge\rho_{\mathrm{crit}}(t)=3/8\pi G t^2$---a
\emph{condensed} void), the patch fills and collapses to that horizon mass: the OGU
becomes a horizon-scale hole and
\begin{equation}
  M_{\mathrm{OGU}}\;\sim\;\frac{c^3\,t}{2G},
  \label{eq:ogu-mass}
\end{equation}
set by its \emph{age}, not the void density. (For a sub-critical void it is
density-limited and smaller.) This is the same ``universe $=$ its own Schwarzschild
radius'' identity that opened the program (\Cref{chap:sims}), now read as a growth
law: an OGU grows until it \emph{is} the black hole of its causal patch. So the OGU
mass measures the supraverse age (\Cref{fig:genesis}, left)---$\sim$our mass at one
Hubble time, $\sim10^{65}\,\mathrm{kg}$ at $\sim10^{12}$ Hubble times. Its sibling
count follows, $N_{\mathrm{sib}}\sim\epsilon\,M_{\mathrm{OGU}}/M_{\mathrm{vis}}$, so
a $10^{65}\,\mathrm{kg}$ OGU has $\sim10^{11}$ universes like ours. Crucially this
leaves our depth untouched: a bigger OGU just means more siblings, and we are BHU1
either way (\Cref{sec:ogu-size}). The OGU mass is therefore genuinely
\emph{open}---it tracks the (unknown) supraverse age---but it is no longer
mysterious: \eqref{eq:ogu-mass} ties it to one number, and decouples it cleanly
from where we sit.

\subsection{The foam, and the average OGU size}
\label{sec:foam-size}

An individual OGU never runs out (\Cref{sec:growth-depth}), but the
\emph{population} does: OGUs nucleate throughout the void and grow at the causal
speed until they \emph{impinge} on their neighbours, tiling the void into a foam.
That collective ``running out of nothing'' fixes a characteristic cell size, and
hence an average OGU mass, in terms of the one number we still lack---the birth
rate. This is the Kolmogorov--Johnson--Mehl--Avrami problem: with OGUs nucleating
at rate $\beta$ (per comoving 3-volume per time) and each growing a horizon of
radius $R=c(t-t_{\mathrm{birth}})$, the void fills at
$t_{\mathrm{fill}}=(3/\pi\beta c^3)^{1/4}$, the characteristic cell radius is
$L\sim c\,t_{\mathrm{fill}}\sim(c/\beta)^{1/4}$, and the average OGU mass is the
horizon mass at impingement,
\begin{equation}
  M_{\mathrm{avg}}\;\sim\;\frac{c^3 t_{\mathrm{fill}}}{2G}
  \;\sim\;\frac{c^2}{2G}\Big(\frac{c}{\beta}\Big)^{1/4}.
  \label{eq:foam-mass}
\end{equation}
Frequent births tile the void into many small universes; rare births let each grow
large before impinging (\Cref{fig:voidfoam}). So ``how big is the average OGU''
\emph{is} ``how often is one born''---the OG nucleation rate of
\Cref{sec:notogu} above, the single unknown $\beta$ to which the OGU mass and the
supraverse age both reduce. Pin $\beta$ (or measure one OGU's size) and the whole
foam scale follows. The Johnson--Mehl tessellation also settles the shape question
of \Cref{chap:viz}: impinged cells are curved polygons, not circles---only an
isolated, not-yet-impinged OGU is round, so circular bubbles in the foam map mark
universes still far from their neighbours.

\subsection{Foam dynamics: equilibrium, continued growth, and whether an OGU dies}
\label{sec:foam-dynamics}

Three questions about the foam, three answers.

\paragraph{Does a finite box reach equilibrium?} Yes, in two senses. Starting from
empty void, nucleation-and-growth fills the box at $t_{\mathrm{fill}}$ and the
Johnson--Mehl partition \emph{freezes}: no later fluctuation finds unclaimed void
to nucleate in, and fresh-void accretion ends once every OGU is hemmed in by its
neighbours. That is the transient ``polygon equilibrium''---a finite catchment,
finite birth probability, and finite growth do settle into a fixed tessellation.
The deeper, eternal equilibrium is thermodynamic (the supraverse as an
entropy-maximising condensation): ongoing births balanced against merger
coarsening, a \emph{stationary} size distribution rather than a frozen one
(\Cref{app:openq}, Q11).

\paragraph{Do packed OGUs still grow?} Yes---but by slower channels than the
out-of-nothing runaway, exactly as one would guess. Once impinged, an OGU has no
fresh void within reach, so the causal-rate growth $M\sim c^3t/2G$
(\Cref{sec:growth-depth}) stalls. Two channels remain. \emph{(i) Expansion-fed
accretion:} if the void carries its own dark-energy-like expansion
(\Cref{app:openq}, Q15c), new void is continually opened between OGUs and they
accrete it, but diluted by the expansion---slow, and slowing. \emph{(ii) Mergers:}
packed universe-holes coalesce, so the foam \emph{coarsens}---fewer, larger OGUs
over time, mass conserved, like grains in a metal or bubbles in a soap froth. Both
are far gentler than the initial runaway. So growth never quite stops; it
decelerates from the $\dot M\sim c^3/2G$ rush of birth to a slow coarsening crawl.

\paragraph{Does an OGU die?} On any ordinary clock, no---but the intuition that
\emph{Hawking must eventually take over once growth halts} is exactly right, and it
turns out to be the engine of coarsening. In the packed foam there is no fresh void,
so each OGU is bathed in its neighbours' Hawking radiation (and the void's own de
Sitter horizon glow---both $\sim T_H$ of a horizon-mass hole,
$\sim10^{-30}\,\mathrm{K}$, \Cref{chap:cartasis}). At that bath an OGU sits right at
$M\simeq M_{\mathrm{crit}}$: balanced. But a black hole has \emph{negative} heat
capacity ($T_H\propto1/M$), so the balance is \emph{unstable}. An OGU a touch larger
than its neighbours is a touch colder, absorbs more than it emits, and grows; a
touch smaller is hotter, emits more, shrinks---and shrinking makes it hotter still,
a runaway to evaporation. So the foam coarsens by \emph{radiative siphoning}: the
big, cold OGUs drink the radiation of the small, hot ones, which evaporate away into
them (\Cref{fig:coarsening})---Ostwald ripening for universe-holes, the radiative
twin of merger coalescence, conserving total mass while driving the foam toward
fewer, larger universes. The catch is the clock: it runs on the Hawking time,
$t_H = 5120\pi G^2M^3/\hbar c^4 \sim 10^{143}$--$10^{178}\,\mathrm{s}$ for OGU masses,
so OGUs are immortal on any sub-Hawking timescale but \emph{not} eternal---a
sub-average universe is, with cosmic patience, eaten by its big neighbour. The
\emph{interior} heat-dies meanwhile (\Cref{chap:timearrow}); the hole persists,
feeding its children's dark sector (\Cref{chap:darksector}), until it is itself
merged or, if it drew the short straw, slowly siphoned away. Whether birth in fresh
void balances this coarsening into a true steady state or the foam coarsens without
bound is the open dynamical content of the supraverse equilibrium
(\Cref{app:openq}).

\subsection{The void is a heat-dead interior: the recursive cycle}
\label{sec:recursive-cycle}

Follow the coarsening to its end. The winners grow, the losers evaporate, and a
surviving OGU eventually exhausts even its own structure: its black holes
Hawking-evaporate, its matter redshifts away, and the interior settles into empty,
cold ($T_{\mathrm{dS}}\sim10^{-30}\,\mathrm{K}$), dark-energy-driven de Sitter
space. A ginormous heat-dead OGU is, gravitationally, \emph{a smooth empty void}---
and that is precisely the substrate from which SCT nucleates OGUs. So it does what
intuition demands: it breeds a fresh foam of OGUs inside itself, and the whole
thing cycles.

\paragraph{The void is not primordial.} This collapses a hidden assumption. The
``void'' of \Cref{sec:genesis-void} need not be a separate arena outside everything;
it is the \emph{asymptotic state every universe reaches}. Universes nest both ways:
we are inside a parent, and our own far future will, in its emptiness, nucleate a
foam of fresh OGUs that seed their own lineages. There is no first void and no last
universe---the supraverse is self-similar and eternal in both directions, with no
first cause required.

\paragraph{Why heat death does not forbid it.} The obvious objection is
thermodynamic: heat death is \emph{maximal} entropy, yet a fresh bounce needs
\emph{low} entropy. Penrose's resolution applies exactly~\citep{penrose2010}. The
\emph{total} entropy (thermal plus horizon) does rise monotonically, obeying the
Second Law. But the entropy a bounce needs small is the \emph{gravitational}
(Weyl/clumping) entropy, and that \emph{cycles}: low at the smooth bounce, high while
matter clumps and black holes form, low again once the holes evaporate and the
interior smooths back to de Sitter (\Cref{fig:cycle}). The de Sitter far future is
conformally flat---Weyl curvature zero---so it is gravitationally smooth, low in the
only entropy that matters for nucleating the next low-entropy bounce. The reset runs
on the black-hole Hawking clock ($\sim10^{100}\,\mathrm{yr}$), the same clock as the
foam coarsening above.

\paragraph{Conformal cyclic cosmology, made to branch.} This is Penrose's
CCC---the smooth, scale-free far future serving as the next aeon's low-Weyl
beginning~\citep{penrose2010}---with one difference: a dead aeon here does not hand
off to a \emph{single} successor by conformal rescaling, but nucleates \emph{many}
OGUs in its emptiness, a whole foam. SCT is CCC made to branch. The birth rate
$\beta$ of \Cref{sec:foam-size} is then the de Sitter vacuum's nucleation rate, and
because the void endures forever, even an exponentially tiny rate eventually
fires---the cycle always closes. One temperature governs the whole far future: the
de Sitter value $T_{\mathrm{dS}}=\hbar H_\Lambda/2\pi k_B\sim10^{-30}\,\mathrm{K}$
sets the nucleation bath, the foam-coarsening bath, and the parent-horizon glow
alike. \emph{(This is a synthesis at the speculative end of the program---a natural
closure of the foam picture, consistent with the gravitational arrow of
\Cref{chap:timearrow}, not a derived result.)} Reproducible as
\texttt{figures/scripts/ch05\_cycle.py}.

\paragraph{Why this rescues typicality.} The cycle also settles a Copernican worry
the framework had quietly incurred. To find ourselves \emph{in} a Big Bang, fourteen
billion years into a universe's life, looks embarrassingly special---a privileged
moment in a privileged place, exactly what the Copernican principle forbids. The
recursive cycle dissolves it. Every universe has its own bounce and its own observers
a few billion years in; there have been, and will be, gazillions of aeons, each a
foam of universes nested inside the heat-dead past of another. We sit not at a
special origin but at a \emph{typical} vantage---a random $\mathrm{BHU}_1$ among
countless near-identical ones, carrying the ordinary imprints of its kind. So the
framework returns the very principle it began by assuming: nothing about our
circumstance need be special, because the supraverse is an eternal, self-similar
screensaver of near-identical beginnings, and ours is simply one lit pixel among the
uncountably many.

\subsection{Infinitely many OGs; do they grow forever?}

By Axiom~\ref{ax:qm} plus infinity, the supraverse nucleates \emph{infinitely
many} OG universes. Whether an individual OG grows without bound is open. In a
static infinite bath the negative heat capacity drives unbounded runaway
accretion; in practice growth is regulated---by depletion of the local
environment, by the void's own expansion diluting the bath, and by the stationary
balance of the foam (large holes growing, new OGs nucleating, sub-critical holes
evaporating). The honest statement is that individual universes can grow very
large while the population remains statistically stationary; the global question
is entangled with the measure problem (Appendix~\ref{app:openq}).

\section{Information structure of the supraverse}

\subsection{Local vs.\ global unitarity}

Each universe, viewed in isolation, appears to lose information through Hawking
radiation. But the Hawking radiation is entangled with the universe's interior
content, and that entanglement extends across bounce membranes.

\textbf{The supraverse as a whole is unitary.} Information that ``disappears''
from a baby universe via Hawking radiation reappears in the parent's exterior.
Information that flows into a parent black hole reappears in the baby's interior.
Total information is conserved across the supraverse. This resolves the
black-hole information paradox at the supraverse level: each universe's internal
unitarity may be approximate, but global supraverse unitarity is exact.

\subsection{Entanglement across membranes}

The persistent Cartasis channel maintains entanglement between parent exterior
and baby interior throughout the parent's lifetime. This is the structural
realization of ER\,$=$\,EPR~\citep{maldacena2013}: the geometric connection
between parent and baby is the entanglement structure between Hawking radiation
and the baby's matter content.

\section{Time within the supraverse}

There is no privileged supraverse-wide time direction. Each universe has its own
internal time arrow (set by its bounce-to-future thermodynamic gradient). These
arrows point in different directions in supraverse coordinates. The supraverse as
a whole is globally time-symmetric: equal numbers of universes with arrows
pointing ``this way'' and ``that way.'' See Chapter~\ref{chap:timearrow} for the
detailed treatment.
