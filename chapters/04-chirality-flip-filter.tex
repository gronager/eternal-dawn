\chapter{Chirality: Flip, Filter, or Both}
\label{chap:chirality}

\section{The question}

When matter crosses Cartasis from parent to baby, what happens to its chirality
(matter vs.\ antimatter content)? Three candidate mechanisms have been
considered. The best current understanding is that two of them operate
simultaneously.

\section{Mechanism A: Flip}

\paragraph{Claim.} The bounce geometry implements a CPT-like operation. Matter on
the parent's side emerges as antimatter on the baby's side (or vice versa).

\paragraph{Physical basis.} The bounce involves a smooth transition where the
radial direction (timelike inside the parent's horizon) becomes the time
direction in the baby. This is a genuine geometric inversion of the time
direction at the crossing. Combined with the parity inversion natural in
spherical bounce geometry, this is a PT operation. By CPT symmetry (a theorem in
any local Lorentz-invariant QFT), PT applied to matter is equivalent to C
applied to matter---i.e.\ conversion to antimatter.

\paragraph{Consequence.} Each generation has opposite chirality bias from its
parent. Lineages alternate matter\slash antimatter\slash matter\slash antimatter
across generations.

\paragraph{Strengths.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Built on CPT symmetry, which is a theorem rather than an assumption.
  \item Preserves total particle\,$+$\,antiparticle content, so every generation
    is viable.
  \item Naturally produces matter--antimatter asymmetry without compounding to
    sterility.
  \item Consistent with the observed near-thermal Planck spectrum of the CMB
    (assuming CMB $=$ parent Hawking radiation, Chapter~\ref{chap:darksector}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Weaknesses.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The ``genuine geometric inversion'' claim is subtle; some readings of the
    bounce geometry do not produce a physical chirality flip but only a
    coordinate relabeling.
  \item Requires the bounce geometry to have specific topological properties that
    may not hold for all bounce solutions.
  \item Hard to verify without detailed Einstein--Cartan bounce calculations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mechanism B: Filter}

\paragraph{Claim.} The torsion--spin coupling at Cartasis density is
chirality-selective. One handedness experiences stronger effective repulsion
than the other, given a background chirality bias. The minority chirality is
preferentially scattered back; the majority chirality passes through.

\paragraph{Physical basis.} The Einstein--Cartan coupling between torsion and
fermion currents goes through the axial current
$J^5 = \bar\psi \gamma^5 \gamma^\mu \psi$, which has definite handedness. At high
spin density with a chirality bias, the effective interaction is asymmetric
between handedness.

\paragraph{Consequence.} Each generation has \emph{enhanced} chirality bias
relative to its parent. The filter compounds: small biases get amplified across
generations.

\paragraph{Strengths.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Cleanly grounded in the explicit form of the Einstein--Cartan Lagrangian.
  \item Provides a mechanism for the strong asymmetry observed in our universe
    (deep-generation enhancement from many filterings).
  \item Can be calculated quantitatively from the Lagrangian.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Weaknesses.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If filter is the \emph{only} mechanism, deep-generation universes approach
    pure-chirality limits where Standard Model physics breaks down (no antimatter
    for QFT loops, broken electroweak structure).
  \item Going up the lineage tree, parents have \emph{less} asymmetry, eventually
    reaching near-symmetric universes that cannot form structure or black
    holes---the ``hell upward'' problem.
  \item The lineage tree cannot get started from such an upper limit, breaking the
    framework's self-consistency.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mechanism C: Statistical inheritance from OG fluctuations}

\paragraph{Claim.} OG universes (those nucleated directly from vacuum
fluctuations rather than descended from parents) inherit their chirality bias
from the \emph{specific} fluctuation that seeded them. The vacuum is symmetric on
average, but individual large fluctuations have nonzero bias drawn from a
statistical distribution.

\paragraph{Physical basis.} A vacuum fluctuation that reaches Cartasis density
and seeds a bounce is a specific quantum state with definite quantum numbers,
not a vacuum average. The chirality bias of this state is drawn from the
fluctuation distribution. By CPT symmetry of the vacuum, equal numbers of
matter-biased and antimatter-biased fluctuations occur.

\paragraph{Consequence.} OG universes come in matched matter\slash antimatter pairs
(statistically). Each OG starts a lineage with its specific bias.

\paragraph{Strengths.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item No baryogenesis mechanism needed; the bias is statistical, not generated.
  \item Preserves CPT symmetry at the supraverse level (equal matter\slash antimatter
    lineages).
  \item Provides initial conditions for the lineage dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Weaknesses.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Says nothing about how the bias evolves across generations within a
    lineage; needs Mechanism A or B for that.
\end{itemize}

\section{The synthesis: Flip $+$ Filter $+$ Statistical inheritance}

The current best understanding is that all three mechanisms operate.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{OG bounces inherit asymmetry from vacuum fluctuations
    (Mechanism C).} Each OG starts with a specific bias.
  \item \textbf{Subsequent bounces flip chirality (Mechanism A).} Lineages
    alternate matter\slash antimatter across generations. This preserves viability
    and avoids the hell-upward problem.
  \item \textbf{Torsion coupling modulates the bias magnitude (Mechanism B).}
    Filter effects refine the bias slightly with each generation, but do not
    dominate the dynamics.
\end{enumerate}

Result: lineages look like alternating matter\slash antimatter signs with slowly
varying magnitudes. Each universe in a lineage is viable. Going up the lineage
tree, you find alternating-sign universes with comparable bias magnitudes, not
progressively-symmetric hellish universes.

\section{The CMB constraint}

The CMB is observed to be very nearly a perfect Planck spectrum (deviations
$< 10^{-5}$). If the CMB is interpreted as parent Hawking radiation crossing into
our universe (Chapter~\ref{chap:darksector}), then a strong filter would produce
specific spectral distortions (rejection of one chirality component during
crossing). The observed near-perfect Planck spectrum constrains the filter to be
weak.

A flip-dominated mechanism, by contrast, preserves the thermal spectrum (CPT
conjugation does not change photon thermality). So observations favor flip over
strong filter. This is consistent with the synthesis: flip is the dominant
generational mechanism, filter is a small modulation.

\section{Implications for matter--antimatter asymmetry}

In the synthesis:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Our observed asymmetry ($\eta \sim 6 \times 10^{-10}$ baryon-to-photon
    ratio) comes primarily from the statistical bias of our OG ancestor's seeding
    fluctuation, propagated through many generations of flip $+$ filter
    modulation.
  \item The ``missing antimatter'' of standard cosmology is not actually missing
    ---it exists in other lineages (matter-biased and antimatter-biased OG
    lineages are equally numerous in the supraverse).
  \item Within our lineage, alternating generations have opposite bias signs. Our
    parent is antimatter-dominated. Our grandparent is matter-dominated (like
    us). Our children will be antimatter-dominated. And so on.
\end{itemize}

\section{Implications for dark matter}

If filter operates even weakly, some matter content from the parent that does
not pass the filter remains at the Cartasis membrane. From the baby's
perspective, this is gravitational influence from ``matter just outside our
cosmological horizon, in the wrong chirality''---which manifests as dark matter
(Chapter~\ref{chap:darksector}).

The dark-matter-to-baryon ratio ($\sim 5\!:\!1$ by mass in our universe) might
be quantitatively predicted by the filter efficiency. If our parent had bias
$b_p$ and the filter passes fraction $f(b_p)$, then the dark-matter fraction is
$\sim (1-f)/f$. A $5\!:\!1$ ratio implies $f \sim 1/6$, meaning the filter
rejects about $5/6$ of what crosses. This is a specific predictable number from
Einstein--Cartan Lagrangian parameters.

\section{Open questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Precise calculation of flip vs.\ filter contributions from a specific
    bounce geometry.
  \item Whether baryon number is exactly conserved at Cartasis, or violated
    through electroweak anomalies.
  \item How quantum-field-theoretic effects (sphaleron processes at trans-EW
    temperatures) interact with the bounce dynamics.
  \item Whether the chirality dynamics depend on the bounce being rotating
    (Kerr-like) vs.\ non-rotating (Schwarzschild-like).
  \item The exact relationship between rotation, chirality, and the
    preferred-axis signature observed in galaxy rotations.
\end{itemize}
