\chapter{The Solitonic Origin of Matter}
\label{chap:solitons}

Part~I took a single refusal---no singularities---and followed it to a cosmology, implemented
by one object: a gravitational potential, extended by torsion, that grows steeply enough at
high density to halt collapse and reverse it (Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce}). This Part takes that
potential at its word in a second place. If a torsion-supported potential is a real feature of
geometry sourced by spin, it does not switch on only when a universe collapses---it is present
wherever spin density is, and a potential that can arrest a universe can also \emph{bind}: it
can hold a finite, self-sustaining knot of field together. The wager of this Part is that those
knots are the elementary fermions.

\begin{conjecture}
If the gravity--torsion interaction is what forbids singularities, then its stable bound
solutions---regular, horizonless, spin-supported solitons---are the elementary constituents of
matter, and their masses are binding energies rather than inputs.
\end{conjecture}

This is the nonlinear-spinor programme of \citet{finkelstein1951}, \citet{heisenberg1957},
and \citet{soler1970}---one nonlinear Dirac equation, one fundamental constant, the whole
particle spectrum---now sourced by Einstein--Cartan torsion rather than an ad hoc
self-coupling. That the torsion four-fermion term is the natural source has been argued before:
\citet{diether2020} use it as the spin-energy that sets the fermion mass scale, and
\citet{tukhashvili2024} show the same torsion interaction can drive chiral symmetry breaking and
a cosmological bounce together---so the ingredients of this Part are not new, and we build on
them. Heisenberg's \emph{Weltformel} foundered on the same two walls we will meet: the absolute
mass scale, and the strongly-coupled bound-state spectrum. The first we dispatch in
Section~\ref{sec:ir-potential}, before any masses are quoted, so that the rest of the Part
works in physical units; the second we reduce to a finite list of lattice calculations
(Chapter~\ref{chap:weltformel} and Appendix~\ref{app:lattice}). What survives in between is a
genuine, if approximate, derivation of the particle spectrum from geometry.

\section{The torsion interaction in the infrared}
\label{sec:ir-potential}

The bounce potential of Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce} is classical, and its coupling is gravity's
own: the Einstein--Cartan four-fermion term carries $\kappa = 8\pi G/c^4$, so a soliton bound by
it alone would sit at the Planck mass $M_{\mathrm{Pl}}c^2=\sqrt{\hbar c^5/G}\approx
1.2\times10^{19}~\mathrm{GeV}$---seventeen orders of magnitude too heavy. Before going further we
must ask the question Heisenberg could not answer: \emph{what is the quantum version of this
potential in the infrared}, where the field condenses and matter forms?

The answer is the mechanism that already sets every strong-interaction scale. A gauge coupling
that is weak in the ultraviolet but asymptotically free runs to strong coupling in the infrared
and condenses there, at a scale set by \emph{dimensional transmutation} \citep{coleman1973}:
\begin{equation}
  \Lambda \;=\; M_{\mathrm{Pl}}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi}{b_0\,g^2}\right).
  \label{eq:transmutation}
\end{equation}
For the spin-gravity sector to run, torsion must propagate---which it does once the
Einstein--Cartan connection is promoted to a dynamical (Poincar\'e) gauge field
\citep{kibble1961,sciama1964,hehl1976}, carrying its own coupling $g$ independent of Newton's
$G$. Equation~\eqref{eq:transmutation} then turns an utterly ordinary coupling, $g^2\sim0.02$
(weaker than electromagnetism), into the observed electroweak scale: $\exp(-40)\approx10^{-17}$
is the exponential of a small number, not a fine-tuning. This is precisely the argument
$\Lambda$CDM and the Standard Model already make for $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, which sits
nineteen orders of magnitude below $M_{\mathrm{Pl}}$ from a coupling of order $1/30$; we make it
once, here, for the spin-gravity field.

This coupling $g$ is emphatically \emph{not} Newton's $G$. It is the strong, mass-giving
coupling that condenses at $\Lambda\sim\mathrm{TeV}$---some $(M_{\mathrm{Pl}}/\Lambda)^2\sim
10^{33}$ times stronger than gravity---and the cosmological bounce of Part~I uses the
\emph{gravitational} coupling, not this one. That the two must be distinct is not an assumption:
a neutron star proves it, since a bounce driven by the strong coupling would occur near nuclear
density, where neutron-star cores sit without bouncing (Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce}). So Part~I's
bounce and Part~II's mass scale are two separate sectors of the one connection, cleanly split by
the cooling between them.

We therefore work, for the remainder of this Part, in units of the condensation scale
$\Lambda\simeq v\simeq 246~\mathrm{GeV}$---the \emph{infrared} scale of the torsion interaction
(Figure~\ref{fig:scalegap}). The Planck scale does not reappear; the masses below come out in
$\mathrm{GeV}$ and $\mathrm{MeV}$. The one number this argument does not yet fix is $\Lambda$
itself (equivalently $g$ and $b_0$): that is the density question of
Chapter~\ref{chap:weltformel} and lattice target~L5 (Appendix~\ref{app:lattice}). With the scale
set, we can solve for the spectrum.

\section{The nonlinear Dirac equation}
\label{sec:nonlinear-dirac}

In Einstein--Cartan geometry the connection carries torsion sourced by fermion spin
(Axiom~\ref{ax:ec}); torsion is non-propagating in the minimal theory and can be eliminated
algebraically, leaving a Dirac field with a quartic self-interaction, the Hehl--Datta term
\citep{hehl1976}:
\begin{equation}
  i\hbar c\,\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu \psi - m c^2 \psi
   \;=\; -\,\frac{3}{2}\,\kappa\,(\bar\psi\,\gamma_5\gamma^\mu\,\psi)\,\gamma_5\gamma_\mu\,\psi .
  \label{eq:nld}
\end{equation}
The binding ``potential'' is not external: it is the well the fermion's own axial-spin density
$j_5^\mu=\bar\psi\gamma_5\gamma^\mu\psi$ digs for itself. This is the decisive break from
single-particle quantum mechanics, and it is worth spelling out the step, because a reader
trained on the hydrogen atom or the harmonic oscillator has never met it: there, the potential
is \emph{given} and the mass is a fixed parameter; here, both are generated by the field itself.

The move is the mean-field (Hartree) factorization of the four-fermion term, exactly as in the
Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model \citep{nambu1961}. Split the bilinear into its vacuum average and a
fluctuation, $\bar\psi\psi = \sigma + (\bar\psi\psi-\sigma)$, and drop the square of the
fluctuation:
\begin{equation}
  (\bar\psi\psi)^2 \;\approx\; 2\sigma\,(\bar\psi\psi) - \sigma^2,
  \qquad \sigma \equiv \langle\bar\psi\psi\rangle .
  \label{eq:meanfield}
\end{equation}
The first term is \emph{linear} in $\bar\psi\psi$---a position-dependent Dirac mass
$g\,\sigma(r)$---so \eqref{eq:nld} becomes an ordinary Dirac equation in a scalar well,
$M(r)=m-g\,\sigma(r)$; the constant $-\sigma^2$ is the condensate's own field energy. The
condensate is not free: extremising the energy, $\delta E/\delta\sigma=0$, fixes it
\emph{self-consistently} by the gap equation
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(r) = g\,\langle\bar\psi\psi\rangle = g\!\!\sum_{\text{filled }n}\!|\psi_n(r)|^2 ,
  \label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}
the well determining the levels and the filled levels sourcing the well, iterated to a fixed
point. This is the Friedberg--Lee soliton bag \citep{friedberg1977}---and, mathematically, it is
the \emph{same} self-consistent gap equation as Bardeen--Cooper--Schrieffer superconductivity:
the chiral condensate $\sigma$ plays the role of the Cooper-pair condensate, the dynamical mass
$g\sigma$ the role of the superconducting gap $\Delta$. Nambu's insight, for which he shared the
Nobel Prize, was precisely that a particle's mass is generated like a BCS gap. Two consequences
follow that single-particle quantum mechanics cannot show but condensed matter shows daily: the
mass is \emph{relative} to the condensate (turn $\sigma$ off and it vanishes), and it
\emph{melts}---above a critical temperature the symmetric solution $\sigma=0$ wins, exactly as a
superconducting gap closes above $T_c$ (the chiral phase structure of the torsion four-fermion
interaction was mapped out by \citet{xue2008}). The condensate is no calculational fiction but a measured
order parameter: in QCD $\langle\bar q q\rangle\simeq-(250~\mathrm{MeV})^3$, its thermal melting
is seen in heavy-ion collisions, and $99\%$ of the proton's mass is condensate field energy
rather than bare quark mass. ``Mass is configurational'' is, for ordinary matter, an
experimental fact. The mean-field treatment is an approximation---qualitatively exact for the
symmetry breaking, quantitatively refined beyond mean field (the lattice, Appendix~\ref{app:lattice})---but
the condensate, the gap, and the melting are real.

That such a bound, regular, finite-energy solution exists in three dimensions, where Derrick's
theorem forbids static \emph{scalar} lumps, is not an oversight: the first-order Dirac kinetic
term scales differently from a scalar's, and the binding balances two terms of different
size-scaling---the same two-terms-balance that makes the bounce. Soler-type solitons are known
to exist for exactly this reason \citep{soler1970}.

\section{Separating the equation: the radial problem}
\label{sec:radial}

The solution proceeds exactly as for the hydrogen atom. The Dirac operator commutes with total
angular momentum, so we separate the spinor into angular and radial parts using the spinor
spherical harmonics $\Omega_{\kappa m}$,
\begin{equation}
  \psi(\mathbf r) = \frac{1}{r}\begin{pmatrix} G(r)\,\Omega_{\kappa m}(\hat r) \\[2pt]
                                    i\,F(r)\,\Omega_{-\kappa m}(\hat r)\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where the integer $\kappa$ collects the orbital and total angular momentum ($\kappa=-1$ for the
ground $s_{1/2}$ state). The angular part integrates out, and the two radial functions
$G,F$---the ``large'' and ``small'' components---obey the coupled first-order system
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dG}{dr} = -\frac{\kappa}{r}\,G + \frac{1}{\hbar c}\big(E + Mc^2 + S(r)\big)\,F,
  \qquad
  \frac{dF}{dr} = +\frac{\kappa}{r}\,F - \frac{1}{\hbar c}\big(E - Mc^2 - S(r)\big)\,G,
  \label{eq:radial}
\end{equation}
with $S(r)=-g\,\sigma(r)$ the self-generated scalar well. Regularity at the origin fixes the
boundary condition $G\sim r^{|\kappa|}$, $F\to0$; normalisability fixes it at infinity. Together
these turn \eqref{eq:radial} into an eigenvalue problem: bound states exist only at a discrete
set of energies $E_n$, exactly as the hydrogen radial equation admits solutions only at the
Balmer energies.

Eliminating the small component gives the equivalent Schr\"odinger-like form for $u=G$,
\begin{equation}
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\,u'' + \Big[\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\kappa(\kappa+1)}{r^2}
      + V_{\mathrm{eff}}(r)\Big]\,u = \mathcal{E}\,u,
  \qquad V_{\mathrm{eff}}\sim M(r)^2 c^4,
  \label{eq:schrodinger}
\end{equation}
a particle in the self-consistent well $M(r)^2$, with the centrifugal barrier $\kappa(\kappa+1)$
the analogue of $\ell(\ell+1)$. The well is not given in advance: the occupied levels source the
condensate through \eqref{eq:nld}, the condensate is the well, and one iterates
$M\!\to\!\{u_n,E_n\}\!\to\!\sigma\!\to\!M$ (the relativistic Hartree loop) to a fixed point. That
loop converges to a genuine self-bound object (Figure~\ref{fig:self-consistent})---the torsiton
exists as the solution of the field's own equations, not as a postulate.

  (levels~$\to$~density~$\to$~condensate~$\to$~well) of Section~\ref{sec:radial} converges to a

  \texttt{sims/src/cartasis\_sims/self\_consistent.py}.}

We call this stationary, self-bound solution the \textbf{torsiton}: where the gravity quantum is
the graviton, the torsion-bound matter quantum is the torsiton, and a torsiton is what an
electron or a quark \emph{is}. (``Soliton'' remains the generic mathematical object; ``torsiton''
names this particular one.)

\section{Mass is configurational}
\label{sec:configurational-mass}

With the wavefunction in hand, the mass follows. The rest energy of the torsiton is the
expectation of the local mass operator in the bound state---an overlap integral of the
wavefunction with the condensate,
\begin{equation}
  m_{\mathrm{torsiton}}\,c^2 \;=\; \int \psi^\dagger\, \beta\,M(r)c^2\,\psi \,d^3r
  \;=\; \int_0^\infty \big(G^2 - F^2\big)\,M(r)c^2\,dr ,
  \label{eq:configmass}
\end{equation}
plus the field energy of the condensate itself, which dominates ($\sim$94\%). Mass is therefore
\emph{configurational}: it is the bound energy of a field configuration, set by how tightly the
knot couples to the surrounding condensate, and it scales linearly with the condensate amplitude
$v$. As $v\to0$ the mass switches off (Figure~\ref{fig:chiral}). This is the analogue of the
constituent mass in the NJL model \citep{nambu1961}: nearly all of the proton's mass is field
energy of this kind, and almost none is the ``bare'' Lagrangian mass.

  \caption{Configurational mass. From \eqref{eq:configmass} the torsiton mass is dominantly
  condensate field energy and is proportional to the symmetry-breaking amplitude $v=f_\pi$:
  turn off the condensate and the mass vanishes. Mass is a property of the bound configuration,
  not an intrinsic tag.}
  \label{fig:chiral}

This is the bridge back to Part~I. What the bounce conserves are $P^\mu$, $Q$, spin, and
$B\!-\!L$---never mass, which is configurational. So the bounce \emph{can} dissolve matter: on
the way into the membrane the condensate melts ($v\to0$, $\Lambda\to0$), mass switches off, and
the field crosses as a hot symmetric plasma; on the way out it re-condenses and re-acquires
weight. Catharsis (Chapter~\ref{chap:bounce}) and genesis (Chapter~\ref{chap:genesis}) are the
two halves of one crossing, and configurational mass is why the crossing is allowed at all.

\section{Generations: an arithmetic ladder, a geometric hierarchy}
\label{sec:generations}

The radial eigenvalue problem \eqref{eq:schrodinger} does not have one bound state but a
\emph{tower} of them, just as the hydrogen atom has $1s,2s,3s$. We \emph{conjecture} that the three
generations of each fermion are the lowest three rungs of this tower---same charge, colour, and
isospin, different radial excitation, with $0$, $1$, and $2$ interior nodes. The tower does not run
forever: the torsiton's well is a \emph{finite} confining bag, rising to a dissociation threshold, so
it binds only a finite number of rungs before a state would unbind. At the derived couplings the count
is small---of order three---and a would-be fourth generation sits at or above threshold and does not
bind. That the bag is finite is what \emph{caps} the generations, and it is why ``three'' is a number
the framework can in principle produce rather than postulate.

The mass of each rung is configurational, Eq.~\eqref{eq:configmass}, and the crucial fact is that the
\emph{size} of the hierarchy is set by one quantity: the \emph{sharpness} of the mass-giving core the
rung couples to. The overlap is exponentially sensitive to that sharpness---a broad core makes every
rung weigh nearly alike (a flat tower), a sharp core spreads the rungs across orders of magnitude. It
is a single function, the same strong-dynamics scale that digs the bag, not a tower of fitted numbers.
And a plausibly sharp core delivers the whole hierarchy from the \emph{consecutive} ladder $n=1,2,3$,
with no exotic radial selection rule: tuning the one core size $s_T$ to the charged leptons reproduces
the entire span---predicted $(0.48,\,121,\,1777)\,$MeV against the observed $(0.51,\,106,\,1777)$, a
total spread of $3685$ versus $3477$, the two intergenerational ratios to $\sim\!20\%$---with the
electron's anomalously tiny mass coming out as a \emph{prediction} once the heaviest rung sets the
scale (Figure~\ref{fig:substrate}, left). One such parameter per tower, read as that tower's grip on
the condensate (its charge and colour), then orders the four towers; the neutrino's lightness is
simply the weakest grip---a colourless, neutral knot---needing no new scale \citep{koide1983}.

Two honest caveats keep this a conjecture rather than a result. First, the sharp core that delivers
the full span is, at present, \emph{fitted}: the minimal mean-field soliton, at the derived couplings,
digs a \emph{broader} core, and there the overlap spread is only $\sim\!50$, not $3477$
(Figure~\ref{fig:substrate}, right). The climb from $\sim\!50$ to the observed span is the deeper
non-perturbative dynamics that sets the bag profile---one lattice-measurable quantity, the condensate
shape around the torsiton (Appendix~\ref{app:lattice}). The hierarchy is therefore not yet
\emph{derived}; it is \emph{reduced} to a single number the lattice can measure. Second, which rung is
the electron is not yet settled. The configurational mass carries two pieces with opposite
rung-dependence: the overlap with the local mass $M(r)$, which is large in the broken \emph{vacuum}
and so weighs the \emph{spread-out} rungs more, and the condensate field energy, which is sourced most
by the \emph{most-localized} rung. The mass \emph{set} and the \emph{span} come out the same either
way; only the assignment---is the electron the ground rung or the most excited?---turns on which piece
dominates, and the same lattice measurement of the condensate profile decides it. (The quarks are
rougher still: the same one-parameter ladders land within a factor $\sim\!3$, with the isospin
splitting between up and down within a generation owed separately.)

So generations remain a \emph{conjecture}---but a sharpened one. The mechanism now carries not only the
right \emph{shape} (the lightest generation anomalously light, the gap widening toward it as in the
leptons) but a demonstrated \emph{route to the full magnitude}, contingent on a single measurable
input: how sharp the torsiton's mass-giving core actually is. The soliton makes the hierarchy
\emph{possible} and reduces it to one number; whether that number comes out right, and whether it is
the electron or the tau that sits at the bottom of the well, the lattice must decide.

We now have the machinery: a self-consistent well, its bound spectrum and wavefunctions, an
overlap integral for the mass, and a tower for the generations---all in units of the infrared
scale $\Lambda$. What remains is to put in the charges that distinguish quarks from leptons, add
the forces, and turn the crank. That is the business of the next chapters; the cooling that
switches it all on, chapter by chapter, is genesis itself.
