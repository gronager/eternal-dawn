# Part III — Electroweak Chemistry

-----

**Status.** Part II proposed that matter is built from gravity–torsion solitons and read the hadron zoo as their chemistry. Part III pushes the same idea into the one sector where it is most exposed: the electroweak interactions — the W, the Z, the Higgs, and the masses they confer. This is the most speculative Part of the book and the most directly falsifiable, and those two facts are the same fact. Where Part I confronts cosmological data it has decades of slack; here the framework walks straight into the most precisely tested theory in physics, where a wrong number is wrong at the fourth decimal. The chapters below state what the soliton picture would have to claim, identify the one place it has a structural advantage over the program it most resembles, and identify with equal clarity the wall that has killed that program before and may kill this one. Nothing here is established. The honest summary is at the end, and it includes a live possibility that this Part is simply wrong.

## A consistency debt, paid first

Before any of the electroweak construction, Part III owes a debt to Part I, and burying it would be the kind of move the book’s own appendix (Q33a) warns against.

Part I, Chapter 2, leans on a specific property of the interaction that drives the bounce. When torsion is integrated out of Einstein–Cartan gravity, the fermion sector is left with the Hehl–Datta four-fermion contact term — the squared axial current — and Part I uses the fact that this term is separately **C-, P-, and T-even** to prove that the bounce cannot manufacture a matter–antimatter asymmetry. That proof is load-bearing for the cosmology: it is why the matter excess must be inherited rather than made at the membrane.

The weak interaction is the opposite of C- and P-even. It violates both *maximally* — it acts only on left-handed fermions and right-handed antifermions, the most chiral structure in the Standard Model. So any claim that “the weak force is the torsion contact interaction” collides head-on with a property the book has already used elsewhere. This is a sharper obstruction than the unitarity problem discussed below, and it must be confronted before, not after, the construction:

> The torsion four-fermion term is C/P/T-even; the weak interaction is maximally C/P-violating. The two cannot be the same object as they stand.

There are only a few honest ways forward, and Part III commits to naming them rather than choosing prematurely. Either (i) the weak interaction is *not* the torsion contact term, and the resemblance of their four-fermion form is a structural rhyme rather than an identity — in which case the electroweak sector is genuinely separate and the rest of this Part is about composite *resonances* of the soliton sector, not about deriving the weak force from torsion; or (ii) the chirality structure of the weak interaction is supplied by the chirality of the soliton itself (a spin-one-half soliton has a handedness), and the torsion term provides only the *strength* and the four-fermion *form*, with the parity violation entering through which handedness of soliton participates — which would have to be shown to be consistent with the C/P/T-even character of the bare contact term, and is not obviously so. The program does not currently know which of these holds, and Part III proceeds under reading (i) as the conservative one: the electroweak bosons are composite resonances of the soliton sector, and “weak from torsion” in the strong sense is left as an open and possibly false conjecture. Reading (ii) is flagged in the targets as the higher-risk, higher-reward alternative.

This is not a tidy opening. It is the honest one, and stating it up front is the price of the cosmology’s own argument in Chapter 2 remaining intact.

## Chapter III.1 — Massive Bosons in a Theory of Matter

*The framework makes fermions and reads the zoo as their chemistry. The W, the Z, and the Higgs are none of those things — they are massive bosons — and that is the problem this Part exists to address.*

Part II drew a clean line: matter is solitons (fermions, spin-sourced); force carriers live in the connection and are not solitons. That line is comfortable for the massless carriers — the photon and the gluon fit the connection sector without strain. It is *not* comfortable for the W (∼80 GeV), the Z (∼91 GeV), and the Higgs (∼125 GeV), which are bosons carrying large masses. They sit in the sector Part II declared mass-free, yet they are heavy. Something has to give, and Part III is the working-out of what.

Two things must be kept separate here, because conflating them is a common error and the book should not commit it:

- **The Higgs mechanism for the W and Z is solid, predictive, and tested.** The W and Z acquire mass because a field with a nonzero vacuum value breaks the weak symmetry; the *ratio* of their masses is fixed by the weak mixing angle and confirmed to high precision. This is real physics, not bookkeeping, and the framework must reproduce it, not overturn it.
- **The Higgs *Yukawa* couplings to fermions are the part that explains nothing.** Each fermion mass is a coupling to the Higgs inserted by hand; the Higgs does not predict the electron’s mass, it is *given* the coupling that reproduces it. The Higgs sees the mass hierarchy; it does not cause it. This is the same unexplained sprinkling Part II’s central target (Yukawa-as-overlap) aims at.

So the framework’s quarrel is not with the measured Higgs — the 125 GeV resonance is as real as the Z, and was observed the same way every short-lived particle is, as a reconstructed resonance in invariant-mass spectra. The quarrel is narrower and legitimate: with the *interpretation* that fermion masses originate in arbitrary couplings to an elementary scalar. Part III asks whether the W, Z, and Higgs could instead be **composite objects of the soliton sector**, with the fermion masses arising as binding overlaps (Part II) rather than as couplings to a fundamental field.

## Chapter III.2 — The W and Z as Composite Resonances

*A strongly-coupled fermion sector makes its own vector resonances. The question is whether the weak bosons are the soliton sector’s version of the same thing.*

The template is QCD itself. The low-energy interaction between pions is a four-pion contact term; it has a unitarity problem at higher energy (the amplitude grows without bound); and the object that resolves it is the ρ meson — a composite quark–antiquark vector resonance that nobody puts in by hand. At low energy the ρ *looks like* a contact coupling; at high energy it unitarizes the interaction by being a real, producible particle. This is vector-meson dominance, and it is the honest analogue of what Part III proposes:

> The W is to the soliton sector’s four-fermion interaction as the ρ meson is to the low-energy pion interaction — a composite vector resonance, not a fundamental gauge field.

A cutoff alone cannot do this job, and it is worth being precise about why, because it kills a tempting shortcut. One might hope the bounce’s density wall (the ρ² torsion repulsion, which caps high density and so high energy) simply truncates the runaway amplitude. It does not unitarize anything: hand-truncating an amplitude is not a unitary operation, and a truncated S-matrix conserves probability no better than the divergent one it replaced. Unitarity is restored only by resolving the contact vertex into real propagating degrees of freedom — which is exactly what a composite resonance is, and exactly what a bare cutoff is not. So the proposal is not “the bounce cuts off the weak interaction”; it is “the soliton sector binds its constituents into the vector resonance that resolves the interaction,” with the bounce density setting the scale at which that resonance sits.

**What the picture buys, if it holds.**

- *Unitarization by real resonances,* not by truncation — the physically correct mechanism.
- *No new arbitrary scale.* The compositeness scale is the soliton size R from Part II’s target #1, which must come out near 1/M_W (∼10⁻¹⁸ m) for the resonance to sit at ∼80 GeV. This is a *check*, not a free parameter — and it is a fourth, independent constraint on R, alongside the hadronic-scale and binding-energy constraints of Part II. If R from the bounce lands near 10⁻¹⁸ m, the constraints are mutually consistent; if it lands elsewhere, the picture is in trouble. Either way it is falsifiable arithmetic.
- *Masses as binding, not as Yukawas.* In a composite sector, masses are binding energies and wavefunction overlaps (Part II), not couplings to an elementary scalar — which is the whole point.

**The Higgs, in this picture, is a composite scalar.** There is a deeper unitarity problem than the four-fermion one, and it is the real reason the Higgs has to exist: longitudinal W–W scattering grows with energy and violates unitarity at around 1 TeV *even with the W in place*, and the Higgs is precisely what cancels that growth. So the Higgs is not optional decoration; it unitarizes the gauge sector’s own bad behaviour. In the composite reading, the analogue is clean: a strongly-coupled sector that makes a composite vector (the W) also makes a composite scalar (the analogue of QCD’s σ/f₀(500) resonance), and that composite scalar plays the Higgs role. This is a **composite Higgs**, and the framework’s sharpened version of “the Yukawa sprinkling is unphysical” is: *the Higgs is real, but it is a composite scalar resonance of the soliton sector, and the Yukawa couplings are binding overlaps, not fundamental constants.*

## Chapter III.3 — The Wall: Precision Electroweak

*This program has a name, a forty-year history, and a graveyard. Part III’s job is to say honestly where the soliton version stands in it.*

The proposal of Chapter III.2 — composite W, Z, and Higgs from a strongly-coupled sector — is **technicolor**, or its modern descendant, composite Higgs. It has been pursued hard since the 1980s, and two pieces of precision data have been strangling it. Honesty requires leading with the wall, not the workaround.

**The S and T parameters.** Electroweak precision measurements (chiefly from LEP) pin the W and Z self-energies to per-mille precision, and any new strongly-coupled sector that makes the weak bosons composite generically shifts them. The shifts are conventionally summarized in two numbers, S and T, and the data demand both be small. Most simple composite models predict an **S parameter too large** — and S is positive and additive, so adding more composite matter only makes it worse. This is the bullet that killed minimal technicolor. The soliton version inherits the same exposure: it must produce a small S, and there is no guarantee it does.

**The 125 GeV Higgs looks elementary.** Its couplings scale with mass exactly as an elementary Standard-Model Higgs predicts; its width is narrow; and the extra resonances a composite sector should bring along near the TeV scale (a composite vector partner, a composite scalar partner) have not been seen at the LHC. A composite Higgs should show *some* deviation from elementary behaviour; so far it looks point-like to the precision available. This does not close the door — the deviations could be below current sensitivity — but it is pressure, and it is pressure in the wrong direction for the program.

So the soliton-electroweak picture is not in open water. It is in a parameter space that data has been narrowing for four decades, and a responsible draft says so plainly.

## Chapter III.4 — The One Structural Advantage: Custodial Symmetry from Conjugation

*If Part III has a result worth the risk, it is this one: a constraint that killed the competing program may be satisfied automatically here, by a property the framework already needed for completely independent reasons.*

The two precision parameters are not equally dangerous. T and S have different protections, and the framework’s standing differs sharply between them.

**T is custodial symmetry, and the framework may get it for free.** The T parameter measures the breaking of a specific approximate symmetry — *custodial SU(2)* — whose physical content is the relation M_W = M_Z cos θ_W, i.e. that the parameter ρ = M_W²/(M_Z² cos²θ_W) equals one. (It is measured at 1.0000 to about four decimals.) Custodial symmetry holds when the mass-giving sector treats the three “W-like directions” as an exact triplet, degenerate before electromagnetism splits them. The Standard-Model Higgs has this by accident, through an SO(4) ≅ SU(2)×SU(2) symmetry of its potential breaking to SO(3). Technicolor models generically *broke* it and died on T.

Here is the framework’s possible advantage. The custodial SU(2) is exactly the symmetry that rotates the components of a fermion–antifermion condensate into one another. A soliton–antisoliton condensate — the same 3 ⊗ 3̄ contraction that gave Part II’s colour singlet and meson — carries a natural SU(2)×SU(2) structure on its pairing, and the **conjugation symmetry** of Part II (the bounce ↔ anti-bounce, matter ↔ antimatter flip) is precisely what would force the two factors to condense symmetrically, leaving the diagonal custodial SU(2) unbroken. In one sentence:

> The custodial SU(2) that protects T ≈ 0 would be the diagonal subgroup left unbroken by the conjugation symmetry acting on the soliton–antisoliton condensate.

If that holds, the framework satisfies the constraint that killed most of technicolor *automatically*, using a brick (conjugation) it was already forced to carry — for antimatter (Part I, Chapter 2), for the antiparticle register (Part II), and for the matter/antimatter selection rules of bound states. A structure that pays rent in four independent places is either deeply right or wrong in a consistent and instructive way; the way to tell them apart is to compute. This is the single most promising result in Part III, and it is still a *conditional* — it requires showing that the Part I conjugation symmetry acts on the electroweak condensate the way custodial symmetry requires, which is a calculation, not yet a demonstration.

**S is not protected, and is where the framework can still die.** Custodial symmetry does nothing for S. The generic estimate for a QCD-like composite sector gives S too large by a factor of a few — the technicolor graveyard. The arithmetic is sharp and worth carrying explicitly, because it is the make-or-break. From the two Weinberg sum rules a resonance-saturated sector gives (SymPy-verified, `sims/.../soliton.py`)

> S = 4π (f_π/M_V)² (1 + M_V²/M_A²),

and a QCD-like spectrum (f_π/M_V ≈ 0.12, M_A/M_V ≈ 1.6) returns **S ≈ 0.25** — squarely above the LEP-era bound of ≈ 0.1, the value that buried minimal technicolor. The single lever that brings it down is making the resonances *heavy relative to f_π*: S < 0.1 requires M_V/f_π ≳ 13, against QCD’s ≈ 8 — a factor of ∼1.6 of “walking,” near-conformal enhancement. So the honest target is not “compute S and hope”; it is “show the anharmonic, minimal soliton sector walks by a factor of ∼1.6.” The framework has three legitimate features that the *dead* models lacked, none guaranteed to be enough:

1. *Anharmonic, non-QCD-like dynamics.* The S estimate assumes a QCD-like spectrum; the Part I bounce is an asymmetric, anharmonic well (the ρ² torsion wall against ρ^(4/3) gravity-plus-degeneracy), which is the kind of “walking” dynamics known to reduce S relative to the naive estimate.
1. *Minimal matter content.* S scales with how much composite matter there is, and the framework is aggressively minimal — one brick. Fewer constituents, smaller S.
1. *Forced resonance scale.* The resonance mass is set by the bounce density rather than chosen, which removes a free parameter the old models tuned — though it cuts both ways, since the framework cannot then tune S down.

The honest verdict, stated as plainly as the wall: **T is a possible triumph (protected by conjugation), S is an unprotected coin-flip the framework must compute and survive.** If the anharmonic, minimal sector suppresses S below the LEP bound, Part III has a live theory prettier than the Standard Model’s Yukawa sprinkling. If it predicts the generic large S, Part III is in the graveyard with everyone else, and the book should say so and retreat to Part II.

## What is forced, what is bet, what is owed

Part III claims less than its ambition and risks more than the rest of the book, and both should be visible at a glance:

- **Forced / solid (and not the framework’s to claim — it is standard physics the framework must respect):** that the W and Z get mass by symmetry breaking with M_W = M_Z cos θ_W; that the Higgs unitarizes longitudinal W–W scattering; that the 125 GeV resonance is real.
- **Suggestive (the right *shape* for the framework’s idea):** that a strongly-coupled soliton sector would make composite vector and scalar resonances (the QCD ρ and σ analogues); that the compositeness scale R is constrained, not free, and could be checked against 1/M_W; that masses-as-binding replaces the Yukawa sprinkling in principle.
- **Owed (the calculations Part III lives or dies by):** whether the Part I conjugation symmetry actually protects custodial SU(2) (the T-parameter result — the promising one); whether the anharmonic, minimal soliton sector suppresses the S parameter below the LEP bound (the dangerous one); and the prior consistency question of whether “weak from torsion” survives the C/P/T-even character of the Hehl–Datta term, or whether the electroweak sector is simply a separate composite sector that rhymes with torsion without being it.
- **Live possibility of being wrong:** unlike Part I (slack against cosmological data) and Part II (a program with room to develop), Part III makes contact with precision measurements that have killed its nearest relatives. It can fail cleanly and soon, and the book should treat that as a feature — a falsifiable Part is worth more than a safe one — and should be prepared to retreat to Part II intact if S kills it. Nothing in Parts I or II depends on Part III surviving.

In one breath, in the book’s idiom: *the framework reads the W, Z, and Higgs as composite resonances of the soliton sector rather than fundamental gauge fields and an elementary scalar, replacing the Standard Model’s hand-inserted Yukawa couplings with binding overlaps — a picture whose hardest constraint (custodial symmetry, the T parameter) may be satisfied for free by the conjugation brick the framework already carries, but whose other hard constraint (the S parameter) is unprotected, has buried this picture’s relatives for forty years, and must be computed before any of this is more than a structurally appealing bet.*

## Computation targets (Part III’s additions to the Part IV punch-list)

In dependency order, building on Part II’s targets 1–3 (the soliton, its relative spectrum, its colour representation content):

1. **The consistency question, first.** Determine whether the weak interaction can be sourced from the torsion sector given that the Hehl–Datta term is C/P/T-even and the weak force is maximally C/P-violating — i.e. decide between reading (i) (separate composite sector, rhyming with torsion) and reading (ii) (chirality from the soliton, strength from torsion). *Success:* a definite, internally consistent answer; reading (i) is the conservative default and does not require resolving this to proceed, but the book cannot claim “weak from torsion” until reading (ii) is either established or abandoned.
1. **Custodial symmetry (the T result).** Show explicitly whether the Part I conjugation symmetry, acting on the soliton–antisoliton electroweak condensate, leaves the diagonal custodial SU(2) unbroken. *Output:* a prediction for ρ = M_W²/(M_Z² cos²θ_W). *Success:* ρ = 1 to the measured precision, falling out of conjugation rather than tuned.
1. **The S parameter (the make-or-break).** Compute S from the soliton sector’s actual resonance spectrum — which requires target #1 of Part II (the anharmonic well shape, since the resonance spectrum depends on the anharmonicity). *Success:* |S| below the LEP bound (∼0.1). *Failure:* generic large positive S — in which case Part III is retracted and the book retreats to Part II.
1. **The compositeness-scale check.** Confirm whether the soliton size R from Part II’s target #1 lands near 1/M_W (∼10⁻¹⁸ m), so that the composite vector resonance sits at the observed W mass. *Success:* mutual consistency of R across the hadronic, binding-energy, and electroweak constraints.
1. **A composite-Higgs signature.** Identify what deviation from elementary-Higgs behaviour the composite reading predicts (modified couplings, a TeV-scale resonance partner) and whether it is consistent with current LHC limits or already excluded. *Success:* a definite, falsifiable prediction the next LHC runs can test.

*The dependency spine across all three Parts now reads: the soliton must exist (Part II, target 1) → its anharmonic spectrum sets the resonance structure → the resonance structure sets S (Part III, target 3) → S decides whether the electroweak picture lives. Everything funnels to the shape of the bounce well. That is where the computation begins, and it is the same well whose existence Part II owes — so a single first calculation, the stable soliton of the Part I potential, is the gate for the entire matter-and-force programme of Parts II and III.*

## Update — computed results (first numerical pass)

*Added after a session of direct computation (`sims/`). The S verdict moved — twice —
and the honest net is: more hopeful than the leading-order graveyard, not yet safe.*

**The leading-order S is the graveyard (computed).** The constituent-fermion loop (mass
*M=gv* from the chiral condensate) gives a positive, UV-finite V−A spectral difference,
so S>0, with the standard value S = N_c/6π per doublet: ~0.16 (one doublet), ~0.32 (two),
and — confirming the earlier warning — ~0.21 for Pati–Salam's N_c=4: **extra colour makes
S worse** (`electroweak_S.py`). Escape needs a ~1.7× walk.

**But the torsion's resonance structure points the other way (the Fierz, GREEN).** S is
killed or saved by the vector/axial resonance splitting, set by the meson-channel
couplings G_V, G_A — so the make-or-break is one ratio, G_A/G_V, fixed by the
interaction's Lorentz structure. Fierzing the Hehl–Datta (axial-axial) term into the
exchange channels with explicit Dirac matrices — *validated exactly* against the V−A
self-Fierz identity and Fierz²=identity — gives

> G_S = +1/4, G_P = −1/4, **G_V = G_A = +1/2**, G_T = 0, so **G_A/G_V = 1 exactly**.

The torsion couples the vector and axial channels *equally* — forced, not tuned — which
is the structural prerequisite for walking (M_a₁→M_ρ, where ρ_V and ρ_A cancel and S→0),
the *opposite* of a QCD-like sector. (Bonus: G_S>0 drives the chiral condensate the
soliton forms; G_T=0.) `fierz.py`.

**The RPA confirms the direction (relative, not absolute).** Resumming the bubbles, in the
walking limit the equal couplings keep S *bounded* while a QCD-like sector blows up:
S_torsion/S_QCD falls to ~0.36 (`rpa.py`). So two independent calculations agree the
torsion is on the *right side* of S. The honest limit: this RPA is one-sided (it has the
vector enhancement that *raises* S but not the full axial/Weinberg catch-up that *lowers*
it), so the *absolute* S<0.1 is **not** established — that needs the full chiral RPA, which
is genuinely lattice-scale.

**Net verdict.** Part III is *favourably placed* on its make-or-break — the
torsion-specific G_A=G_V is exactly the feature generic technicolor lacks — but not proven
safe. The absolute S<0.1 is owed and undecided. And a striking consistency check fell out:
the leading-order S would forbid even *one* generation, so walking is *required* by our own
existence (Part II's generation cap), and the torsion supplies it. So Part III's survival
and the existence of three generations are the same question, and both turn on whether the
torsion walks far enough — the one number the full chiral RPA (lattice) still owes.

**Consistency debt update.** The C/P/T-even Hehl–Datta term vs the maximally C/P-violating
weak interaction (flagged at the top) is *not* resolved by the above; the Fierz/RPA work
sits in reading (i) — the electroweak bosons as composite resonances that *rhyme* with the
torsion four-fermion form — and "weak from torsion" in the strong sense remains the open,
possibly-false conjecture it was flagged as.

### Confinement update — the condensate confines, and does double duty

A coda to the strong-sector picture, computed (`flux_tube.py`). The overlap forces
between colour sources all screen (→0); confinement (V~σr) is not a pairwise overlap but
a flux tube of the substrate. If the gravity–torsion vacuum is a dual superconductor, its
condensate expels colour-electric flux into a Nielsen–Olesen string of tension σ ∝ v² =
f_π² (at the BPS point σ = 2π v² exactly, the validation). So the **same condensate scale
that confers (configurational) mass also sets the confinement string tension** — one
substrate, mass and confinement both. This is conditional on the dual-superconductor
realisation, which is a lattice question (Part IV addendum). It strengthens, but does not
resolve, Part III: the electroweak resonance scales and the confinement scale now share a
single origin (the condensate v=f_π), tightening the picture, while the two hard numbers
— the absolute S and whether the vacuum truly confines — remain the owed, lattice-scale
make-or-breaks.
