# 05 — The Dark Sector: Dark Matter, Dark Energy, and the CMB

## Overview

Three of the most puzzling features of observational cosmology — dark matter, dark energy, and the CMB — receive unified interpretations in the SCT framework. All three are signatures of the supraverse touching our universe through the Cartasis membrane.

## Dark matter as parent’s gravitational influence

### The mechanism

The Cartasis membrane persists throughout the parent’s lifetime, with new matter continuously falling through it from the parent’s exterior. The matter content on the parent’s side of the membrane exerts gravitational influence on our universe through the membrane, even though it doesn’t enter our 3D space as ordinary matter.

This gravitational influence manifests as:

- Halo-like distributions around galaxies (where mass concentrated at bounce time)
- Large-scale structure correlations (parent’s mass distribution projected through the bounce)
- Anomalously high rotation velocities of galaxies (additional gravitational binding)
- Gravitational lensing without visible mass

This matches the phenomenology of dark matter.

### Specifically: filter-rejected antimatter

If the bounce has any filter character (chirality-selective transmission, see `03-chirality-flip-filter.md`), then the rejected chirality content remains at the Cartasis membrane. From our perspective:

- The rejected content is gravitationally present (it has mass-energy)
- It doesn’t appear in our 3D space (it’s geometrically at the membrane, not in our interior)
- It doesn’t interact electromagnetically with our matter (no normal photon exchange across the membrane)
- It doesn’t annihilate with our matter (geometric separation prevents direct contact)

**This is the standard phenomenology of dark matter.** The “missing antimatter” of standard cosmology and the “dark matter” of observational cosmology may be the same phenomenon.

### Quantitative prediction

If filter efficiency is $f$ (fraction of input matter that passes), then dark matter to baryon ratio is approximately $(1-f)/f$. Our observed ratio of ~5:1 implies $f \sim 1/6$. This is a specific number that should be calculable from Einstein-Cartan parameters.

If the calculation gives $f \approx 1/6$ from first principles, that’s a strong validation. If it gives something very different, the framework needs revision.

### Predictions distinguishing this from particle dark matter

1. **Dark matter has chirality structure (specifically, opposite to our matter).** Probing dark matter weak-interaction couplings (if any) should reveal antiparticle-like behavior, not particle-like.
1. **Dark matter distribution should track parent’s matter structure, not internal baby dynamics.** This might explain anomalies in dark matter halo profiles vs. CDM predictions.
1. **Dark matter density should evolve with cosmic time** as the parent’s accretion history changes. Tensions in dark matter inferred at different redshifts (e.g., $\sigma_8$ tension) might be evidence for this.
1. **No dark matter particles exist as fundamental constituents.** Direct detection experiments will continue to find nothing. The dark matter is geometric, not particulate.
1. **Gravitational waves should propagate normally** (they’re features of our spacetime), but might show subtle dispersion effects from membrane-mediated coupling at very low frequencies.

## Dark energy as parent accretion dynamics

### The mechanism

The parent black hole, embedded in its own universe, accretes from its surroundings. As parent mass grows, the Cartasis membrane geometry changes — specifically, the membrane’s “size” relative to baby interior grows.

From inside the baby, this manifests as additional expansion pressure beyond what internal baby dynamics would predict. The acceleration of cosmological expansion (attributed to dark energy in standard cosmology) is actually the baby’s response to parent growth.

### Predictions distinguishing this from a cosmological constant

1. **Dark energy is not constant.** It tracks parent’s accretion history, which is generically non-constant.
1. **Dark energy will eventually reverse.** When the parent enters Hawking-dominated regime (mass loss exceeds accretion), the Cartasis membrane shrinks and the baby’s expansion decelerates, eventually contracting. This is a long-term prediction not relevant on observational timescales now.
1. **Recent dark energy evolution.** Current observations (DESI 2024 and related) hint at evolving dark energy with $w_0 w_a$ parameters slightly inconsistent with strict $w = -1$. This is potentially consistent with parent accretion dynamics.

## The CMB as parent Hawking radiation

### The identification

The Cartasis membrane is the same geometric surface as:

- The parent’s event horizon (from outside)
- The baby’s cosmic horizon at maximum lookback (from inside)
- The Big Bang “surface” in standard cosmology

Hawking radiation is emitted at the parent’s event horizon. From the baby’s interior, this same radiation appears at the cosmic horizon — i.e., from every direction at maximum cosmic lookback distance.

**This matches the observed CMB:** thermal radiation from every direction at maximum cosmic lookback.

### Temperature and spectrum

Hawking temperature for a black hole of mass $M$:
$$T_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}$$

For our universe’s parent mass ($\sim 10^{53}$ kg), Hawking temperature is essentially zero at emission. But the radiation has been redshifting through our cosmic expansion, so observed temperature is what we measure: 2.7 K.

The redshift factor depends on detailed bounce geometry. Calculation needed to verify consistency.

### Consistency with observations

The CMB has:

- Near-perfect Planck thermal spectrum (consistent with Hawking thermality)
- Near-perfect isotropy (consistent with bounce surface being homogeneous across the parent’s horizon)
- Tiny anisotropies at $10^{-5}$ level (consistent with bounce-surface inhomogeneities and small parent rotation effects)
- Acoustic peaks in the power spectrum (these need bounce-cosmology rederivation, not yet done)

The standard cosmological explanation of the CMB (recombination at $z \sim 1100$) fits the spectrum and acoustic features beautifully. The bounce-cosmology reinterpretation needs to reproduce these features, which is non-trivial. **This is the central calculational challenge for the SCT framework.**

### Preferred axis

If our parent is rotating (Kerr-like rather than Schwarzschild-like), the rotation axis imprints anisotropy on the bounce surface, which manifests as a preferred direction in the CMB and in subsequent structure formation.

Observational hints of preferred axes:

- “Axis of evil” in CMB low multipoles (alignment of quadrupole and octupole with each other and with the ecliptic)
- Recent JWST galaxy rotation asymmetry (Shamir 2025): ~2:1 ratio of clockwise vs. counterclockwise rotation in JADES, statistically significant at 3.39σ, peaks near Galactic poles

Both could potentially be signatures of parent rotation. The exact relationship between parent angular momentum and observed cosmic anisotropies is calculable but not yet done in detail.

## Hubble tension

The Hubble tension is a persistent ~$5\sigma$ disagreement between $H_0$ measured from CMB (~67 km/s/Mpc) vs. from local distance ladder (~73 km/s/Mpc). Standard cosmology has no clean resolution.

In SCT, the early universe (near the bounce) is dominated by bounce dynamics rather than standard inflation + radiation-dominated phase. The relationship between early-universe expansion and late-universe expansion is modified. This could in principle resolve the Hubble tension, but the specific calculation hasn’t been done.

If the framework can produce the right $H_0$ from late observations while also matching the CMB precisely, this is a major validation. If it can’t, the framework needs revision.
