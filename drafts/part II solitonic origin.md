## The thesis, in one conditional

Part I made a single refusal — no singularities — and followed it to a cosmology. That refusal was implemented by one object: a gravitational potential, extended by torsion, that grows steeply enough at high density to halt collapse and bounce it. Everything in Part I is the working-out of what that potential does to a *universe*.

This Part takes the potential at its word in a second place. If a torsion-supported potential exists at all — if it is a real feature of geometry sourced by spin, and not merely a cosmological convenience — then it does not switch on only when a universe’s worth of matter collapses. It is present wherever spin density is present. And a potential that can arrest the collapse of a universe can also, in principle, *bind* — it can support stable, finite, self-sustaining knots of field. The conditional is therefore:

> *If the gravity–torsion potential is what forbids singularities, then its stable bound solutions — regular, horizonless, spin-supported solitons — are candidates for the elementary constituents of matter.*

This is a hope with a long pedigree (Wheeler’s geons, Burinskii’s Dirac–Kerr–Newman electron, the soliton tradition generally) and a set of well-known walls. Part II’s task is to state the conditional precisely, walk it up to each wall, and report honestly which walls fall, which stand, and which are simply not yet tested. The single result that would make the program worth more than its aesthetics — recovering particle masses as binding energies rather than inputs — is named at the end as the calculation the program owes, not as a result it claims.

A note on the bounce density. Part I sets the cosmological bounce density at ρ_C ∼ 10⁵⁰ kg m⁻³ by balancing torsion against gravity for bulk fermionic matter at roughly one unit of spin per nucleon mass. That figure governs the collapse of a *universe’s* worth of matter. Whether the density at which a *single particle-scale* knot stabilizes is the same number is not established here; the spin configuration of a self-bound core is not obviously the fluid-averaged spin density of collapsing matter, and the relevant coefficient may differ. Throughout Part II, ρ_C denotes the operative bounce/binding density of the regime under discussion, and the question of whether the particle-scale and cosmological-scale values coincide is left explicitly open (Appendix, and the computation targets below). The working expectation — that they are the same physics in two regimes — is stated as an expectation, not a result.

## Chapter II.1 — The Soliton

*A particle, in this reading, is not a point with properties hung on it, but a standing knot of the same field that bounces a universe.*

In Einstein–Cartan geometry the connection carries an antisymmetric part, torsion, sourced by fermion spin (Part I, Axiom A3). Curvature pulls matter together; torsion, at high spin density, pushes it apart. Part I used the second to reverse a collapse. The same opposition admits a stationary solution: a configuration in which inward curvature and outward torsion balance not momentarily, at a bounce, but permanently, holding a finite, localized lump of field together. We call such a stationary solution a **gravity–torsion soliton**, and the proposal of this Part is that the elementary fermions are solitons of this kind.

A name for the quantum. "Soliton" is generic — it names *any* self-bound, non-dispersing solution of a nonlinear field, of which there are many across physics. The object here is the specific one: the stable bound solution of the gravity–torsion field, the torsion-supported standing knot. Where the gravity quantum is the *graviton*, we call the torsion-bound matter quantum the **torsiton** — torsion + the particle suffix, parallel to graviton, naming *which* soliton it is. The torsiton is the elementary unit of matter in this Part: a torsiton is what an electron or a quark *is*, in the same sense that a photon is what light is. (Graviton being already spoken for, torsiton takes the adjacent slot it leaves open.) We lead with "soliton" where the generic mathematical object is meant and "torsiton" where the physical matter-quantum of this framework is meant; the two are introduced together here and used with that distinction throughout.

**Mass is bound field energy, not a separate substance.** The soliton has no point of infinite density at its centre — that is the whole inheritance from Part I’s refusal — so its energy is finite, and its rest mass is simply the total energy of the bound configuration: curvature energy, torsion energy, and the internal motion of whatever the knot contains. There is no mass added by hand; mass is what the bound field weighs. This is the same accounting by which the proton’s mass is overwhelmingly the energy of its internal fields and motion rather than the rest masses of its constituents, and that parallel is not decorative — it is the feature the program most wants to inherit, and we return to it in Chapter II.3.

**The soliton is a fermion, and this is the one genuinely forced property.** Torsion couples to spin. A stationary torsion-supported knot is therefore intrinsically a configuration of nonzero spin, and the minimal such object carries spin one-half. This is the strongest claim in Part II precisely because it is the least optional: the soliton is fermionic *because* the binding mechanism is spin-sourced. The fermionic character of matter is not an input to this picture but a consequence of which field does the binding. (The corollary — that bosons are not solitons of this kind but excitations of the connection itself — is taken up in Part III, where it becomes the dividing line between matter and force.)

**No horizon, hence no baldness.** The classic objection to “particle as black hole” is the no-hair theorem: a black hole remembers only mass, charge, and angular momentum, while a particle carries colour, flavour, weak isospin — a whole register GR cannot store. The objection is fatal to a *horizon*; it is silent on a *horizonless soliton*. Because the bounce removes the singularity, the soliton need have no horizon, and a horizonless regular configuration is under no obligation to forget its quantum numbers. The price is exact and worth stating plainly: this picture buys the right to carry internal structure by giving up the horizon, so a soliton is emphatically *not* a small black hole. It is the regular core that the bounce permits where a black hole’s singularity used to be. The relation to the black-hole limit survives only at the very top of the mass scale, where a soliton’s would-be Schwarzschild radius grows to meet its core; for any real particle that correction is of order (m/m_Planck)² ∼ 10⁻⁴⁰ and does nothing.

**Status of this chapter.** The existence of a stable, regular, spin-supported soliton of the Einstein–Cartan field equations is *assumed* here and is a nontrivial dynamical claim. The soliton tradition (notably Burinskii’s regularized Dirac–Kerr–Newman construction) makes it plausible but not settled in this specific torsion-bounce setting. Establishing that the Part I bounce potential admits stationary bound solutions — and computing even one — is the foundational computation target of Part II (see the targets list). Until it is done, the soliton is a well-motivated hypothesis, not a demonstrated object.

**The first wall is Derrick’s theorem, and the framework is built to clear it.** The standard reason “particle as a lump of field” attempts die is Derrick’s theorem: in three space dimensions a static, finite-energy soliton of a plain scalar field is forbidden by a scaling argument — shrink it and the energy only drops, so nothing holds it at a finite size. Two ingredients evade it, and the soliton has both. First, the binding object is a *Dirac* field, not a scalar: integrating torsion out of Einstein–Cartan leaves a nonlinear Dirac equation of Soler type, whose first-order spinor kinetic term scales differently from a scalar’s, and Soler-type solitons are known to exist in 3-D precisely for this reason. Second, the binding balances *two terms with different size-scaling* — inward attraction against the outward ρ² torsion wall — which is the same two-terms-balance that makes the cosmological bounce; a balance at a finite radius is exactly what Derrick forbids a single scalar and permits two competing powers. So Derrick is not a wall the soliton runs into blindly; it is the wall the framework’s own bounce mechanism is shaped to clear, and confirming it explicitly for the Part I potential is the first item of target #1.

## Chapter II.2 — What a Soliton Must Carry

*The discipline of this chapter is Mendeleev’s: count only the properties the structure forces, mark the rest as inputs or as gaps, and never paint an input to look like a derivation.*

A soliton is not yet a particle until we know what labels it carries. The temptation is to claim the whole Standard Model register falls out of the geometry. It does not. The honest inventory separates cleanly into three bins, and most of the register is in the second and third.

**Forced by the structure.**

- *Spin one-half.* As Chapter II.1: the soliton is fermionic because torsion is spin-sourced. The single most forced property.
- *A conjugation, or matter-sign.* The bounce has an orientation (Part I, Chapter 2: the spin of a seed chooses matter or antimatter at the membrane). A particle-scale soliton inherits the same binary: a core and its time-reversed mirror, the antiparticle. This is a discrete two-valued label, conserved separately from everything else.

**Forced into existence, but input as a structure.**

- *A three-valued internal label (colour-like SU(3)).* Here is the one place a real argument forces something nontrivial, and it is worth giving exactly. The soliton is an identical fermion (spin one-half, Chapter II.1). Suppose the proton-like baryon is a bound state of three such solitons in a spatially symmetric, spin-aligned ground configuration. The Pauli principle forbids that configuration unless the solitons differ in some *additional* internal label that can be made totally antisymmetric across the three — and the minimal group admitting a totally antisymmetric three-body singlet is SU(3) in its fundamental. This is, beat for beat, the historical argument by which colour was first forced (the Δ⁺⁺ problem). So *if* baryons are three-soliton bound states, a three-valued internal label is required — not chosen for elegance, but demanded by Pauli applied to the framework’s own fermionic brick.
  
  Two honest caveats keep this from being more than it is. First, it assumes baryons are three-soliton states, which is itself part of the program’s bet, not an independent fact. Second — and this corrects an overreach the conversation behind this draft made — this colour SU(3) is *not* the same object as the accidental SU(3) of a three-dimensional harmonic core (the Jauch–Hill symmetry of the oscillator’s spatial degeneracy). That spatial symmetry is internal and, as Chapter II.3 argues, unobservable in isolation; colour is an observable charge that sources the strong interaction. They are different SU(3)s and must not be conflated. What is forced is the *existence* of a three-valued antisymmetric label; identifying its detailed representation content with QCD colour is a target, not a result.

**Genuine inputs.**

- *Electric charge,* a continuous U(1). It cannot be reduced to the conjugation sign (a discrete two-valued flip cannot generate a continuous, fractionally-quantized conserved charge; the neutron alone — nonzero matter-sign, zero charge — kills the identification). Charge is flipped *by* conjugation but is not equal to it. It is an irreducible input, and the fractional quantization (charge in thirds) is plausibly tied to the three-ness of the colour label, in the same way it is in the Standard Model.
- *Weak isospin,* an SU(2) doublet label. Part of its structure — that the weak interaction acts on one handedness — connects to chirality, which a spin-one-half soliton possesses natively. But the doublet label itself (that the neutrino and electron are distinct states while both left-handed) is orthogonal to handedness and is a genuine input. This is the seam that forces a Higgs-like bridging field, and it is the business of Part III.

**A dark internal ladder.** A lone soliton’s internal excitations — the rungs of its own gravity–torsion potential — are radiatively inaccessible. A spherically symmetric breathing of a single core changes only its monopole moment, and monopole transitions emit neither a photon (E0 is forbidden) nor a graviton (there is no monopole or dipole gravitational radiation). A single soliton is therefore *radiatively dark to both forces*: it cannot externalize a change in its internal state. The consequence is structural and runs through the rest of the Part: **the internal ladder of a single soliton is not an observable spectrum, and the elementary particles cannot be single excited solitons.** Anything observable must involve at least two solitons, whose relative motion carries a dipole and can radiate. The picture is forced toward composites — and that is the subject of Chapters II.3 and II.4.

**The quarantined gap.** The three-generation hierarchy — electron, muon, tau, identical but for masses spaced geometrically (factors of order 10–200, and not even uniform across the lepton and quark towers) — is *not* in this inventory. No ladder in the framework produces geometric spacing; the harmonic and relativistic spectra of Chapter II.3 give arithmetic or square-root spacing. Generation structure is left explicitly unexplained, exactly as it is in the Standard Model, where it lives in the unexplained Yukawa couplings. The program quarantines it rather than papering over it. This is a feature of honesty, not a hidden weakness: the flavour problem is no one’s solved problem, and a framework that *claimed* to solve it cheaply should be distrusted on sight.

## Chapter II.3 — Mass as Motion

*The most important fact about a composite’s mass is that it is not the sum of its parts — it is the sum plus the energy of their confinement, and for a tightly bound system that second term is almost the whole of it.*

The invariant mass of a bound pair at rest is the sum of the constituents’ *total* energies — kinetic and potential included — not the sum of their rest masses. Internal motion adds to mass. This is not a subtlety of the framework; it is special relativity, and it is the reason the proton weighs ∼938 MeV while the rest masses of its quarks sum to under ten: roughly ninety-nine percent of the proton’s mass is internal motion and binding energy. If the elementary fermions are solitons, then composites built from them inherit this exactly, and it is the feature the program most wants: **most of the mass of matter is the energy of confinement, not the weight of constituents.** A theory that gets this right gets the dominant fact about hadron masses for free, the moment it takes the relativistic bound state seriously.

**Two ladders, not one.** A bound system of solitons carries two distinct quantum numbers, and keeping them apart is the whole game.

- The *internal* ladder: which soliton (which rung of a core’s own gravity–torsion potential). This is the dark, unobservable ladder of Chapter II.2 — stiff (set by ρ_C, the binding density) and frozen on the energy scales of ordinary transitions.
- The *relative* ladder: how the constituents orbit one another — the soft, observable spectrum of the bound state, the analogue of the 1S, 2S, 1P… tower of positronium or charmonium.

The observed spectrum is the *product* of these two ladders, and that product structure is why the real particle spectrum looks irregular while being generated by something simple. A grid viewed at an angle reads as noise. Crucially, by Chapter II.2 the internal ladder is dark, so the *measurable* spectrum is the relative ladder alone, dressed by the (frozen) choice of which solitons are in play.

**The relativistic ladder is Regge-shaped, and that is a bridge, not a coincidence.** The non-relativistic harmonic core gives an equally-spaced ladder (energy linear in the quantum number, with the spatial SU(3) degeneracies 1, 3, 6, 10). But at binding densities the binding is comparable to the mass, and the correct treatment is relativistic — the Dirac-oscillator form, in which it is the *square* of the energy that is linear in the quantum number, so masses grow as the square root of the level and the spacing bunches as one climbs. Equivalently, m² grows linearly: the Regge pattern of real hadrons, with its empirical slope of about 1.1 GeV⁻². The relativistic soliton bound state therefore wears, natively, the empirical signature of the composite hadron spectrum. This is encouraging and must be kept in proportion: Regge behaviour is already claimed, and well, by QCD flux tubes. Reproducing a known slope from a new mechanism is suggestive of consistency, not of superiority; distinguishing the soliton account from the flux-tube account would require a *second* number — a deviation, a curvature, an intercept — where the two disagree. That comparison is a target, not a result.

A first numerical pass already shows the shape. Solving the radial Dirac equation in the self-generated confining well (the SymPy-verified reduction, `sims/.../soliton.py`) returns a rising tower whose squared energies are linear in the level index to better than one percent for a linear (flux-tube-like) wall — the Regge form, parameter-free — and bend upward for the steeper, anharmonic ρ²-wall of the actual bounce. So “the relativistic bound state is Regge-shaped” is now a computed statement and not only an analytic hope. But two honesties bound it. The slope is a *shape*, fixed here in dimensionless units; the absolute scale is the open density question, and the bare Einstein–Cartan coupling would put it near the Planck mass unless the particle-scale binding density differs from the cosmological ρ_C (target #5). And — the point that matters most for what comes after — **this is not an ordinary potential problem.** The well is not external: it is the one the fermion’s own axial-spin density digs through the four-fermion (Hehl–Datta) term, so the honest equation is a *nonlinear, self-consistent* Dirac equation (NJL/Soler class), in which the spectrum back-reacts on the well that binds it and Pauli filling of the levels matters. The fixed-well calculation here is the first iteration of that self-consistency, not its endpoint; the real difficulty — and the place the optimistic analytic story is most likely to get nastier — lives in the back-reaction, and it is owed.

**Relativity dresses the symmetry; it does not preserve it intact.** The same relativistic treatment that produces the Regge form also produces a large spin-orbit splitting (of order the level spacing itself, not the feeble fine-structure scale of atoms), which breaks the clean 1/3/6/10 spatial degeneracy into interleaved sub-ladders and produces an approximate degeneracy between states of different orbital angular momentum — the pseudospin pattern observed in nuclear single-particle spectra. That an unforced near-degeneracy of this kind shows up in nature is a point in the picture’s favour; whether the *specific* Part I bounce profile lands in the coupling class that produces it is a computation (it turns on the Lorentz character of the Cartan term), and is owed.

**Tunnelling gives every state a width.** No finite potential confines absolutely; the bounce is a turning point at finite density, not an infinite wall, so there is always a small amplitude to tunnel through it. Two consequences follow. Inward, a soliton has a finite amplitude to penetrate its own bounce — so a soliton is metastable, with a lifetime, rather than eternal. Outward, a constituent can tunnel out of its coalition — ordinary quantum decay. The unified statement is that a soliton or bound state is not a sharp mass eigenvalue but a resonance with a width set by its tunnelling rate; the sharp, long-lived particles (electron, proton) are those whose barriers are effectively infinite, and the rest of the zoo carries widths. That real hadron resonances all carry widths, and that the stable/unstable distinction tracks binding depth, is the qualitative prediction this makes — correct in shape, and a target to make quantitative.

**The two tunnelling directions are death and birth, and they close the loop to Part I.** The barrier has two sides, and they are not symmetric. *Outward* tunnelling — a quantum escaping the well to lower density — is decay; for a horizon-scale object it is Hawking radiation, the slow evaporation Part I already uses. *Inward* tunnelling is the strange one the cosmology forces us to take seriously: an amplitude to penetrate to densities *above* ρ_C, past the torsion wall. There is nothing classically allowed there — but a region driven past the bounce density is exactly a region that *bounces*, and on the far side that is a new expanding interior. So inward tunnelling is not annihilation into nothing; it is **nucleation** — the same baby-universe birth that Part I’s instanton describes (Chapter 3), now read as WKB penetration of the very same membrane. The membrane is therefore a two-way quantum barrier: *evaporation* outward, *creation* inward. The asymmetry between the two rates is enormous and is the whole reason the world looks stable — outward emission is a single-quantum process, while inward nucleation must assemble a coherent super-Cartan seed and is suppressed by the full instanton action (the mountain-mass exponent of Chapter 3, ∼10^{84}), so every soliton is in principle metastable against nucleating a child universe at a rate of effectively never. Death is easy and birth is instanton-rare; the same wall sets both. (In the strictly *homogeneous* cosmological potential the inward wall rises without bound toward a→0 and the amplitude is only the bounce’s quantum fuzz; it is the *local* collapse — a soliton, or a black hole — that turns inward tunnelling into an actual new region. That distinction is the same one that separates Part I’s OGU instanton from its black-hole children.)

## Chapter II.4 — Chemistry

*The particle zoo is not a ladder of elementary objects. It is chemistry: bound states of a small number of solitons, glued by the residual of the binding force, some configurations stable and some barely bound.*

The “endless discovery of new particles” that motivated this whole line of thought has a historical resolution: the hadron zoo of the 1960s was not a list of elementary objects but a spectrum of resonances — excited and bound states of a few constituents. The framework reads the zoo the same way. The elementary objects are the solitons of Chapter II.1; the observed particles are their *coalitions*, and the organizing force between coalitions is the *residual* of the gravity–torsion binding — the same kind of leftover force that, in the Standard Model, makes the nuclear force out of residual colour (the “van der Waals force of QCD”). The framework’s claim is that this residual force is the residual of the soliton–soliton interaction, and that the molecular reading of the modern exotic states (the loosely-bound hadronic-molecule interpretations of states like X(3872), the doubly-charmed tetraquark, the pentaquarks) is the same physics on the largest hadronic scales.

This reorganizes the spectrum into three layers, and the reorganization is the payoff:

1. The **clean constituent ladder** (Chapter II.3) is not the observed mass spectrum. It is the spectrum of the solitons — the “atoms.”
1. The **zoo** is chemistry: bound states of solitons, glued by the residual force, some deep-well (stable) and some shallow-well (resonances with widths, per the tunnelling argument). “Some states very stable, some less” maps onto binding-energy depth, not onto a fundamental ladder.
1. **Gauge structure** — the photon, gluon, and weak bosons — is not built from solitons at all. It lives in the connection the solitons couple through, and is the subject of Part III.

**Selection rule for radiation.** A change in a coalition’s internal arrangement externalizes a photon only if it changes an electromagnetic multipole of dipole order or higher; a purely monopole (breathing) change is dark (Chapter II.2). So radiation lives in the *relative* coordinates of coalitions, never in a lone core — which is why a nuclear gamma transition is allowed (the rest of the coalition absorbs the recoil and provides the dipole) while a free soliton cannot absorb a photon to climb its own ladder. Mass-from-light is correspondingly always a three-body event: pair production of a soliton and anti-soliton requires a spectator to conserve momentum, never a lone photon condensing. These are the same momentum-conservation fact stated three ways, and they are consequences, not new assumptions.

**The central owed calculation: Yukawa couplings as overlap integrals.** Here is the result that, if delivered, would make the program worth more than its coherence — and here is the honest statement that it has not been delivered. In the Standard Model, a fermion’s mass is its Yukawa coupling times the Higgs vacuum value, and *every Yukawa is a free parameter inserted by hand*. That sprinkling of unexplained coefficients is the ugliest, least-explained corner of the Standard Model, by its own authors’ account, and it is precisely the corner this program aims at. The claim the program *hopes* to establish is that what the Standard Model writes as an arbitrary Yukawa coupling is, in the soliton picture, a **wavefunction overlap integral** between bound solitons — a number computed from the geometry of the binding, not inserted. If a single fermion mass ratio could be recovered, parameter-free, as such an overlap, the picture would have done something the Standard Model cannot.

It has not been done. There is at present no computed soliton wavefunction, no computed overlap, and therefore no recovered mass. This is the program’s defining target, and Part II’s role is to specify it precisely enough that Part IV can attempt it, not to claim it. Until an overlap is computed and compared, “Yukawa as overlap” is an aspiration with a clear mechanism, and is labelled as such.

## What is forced, what is bet, what is owed

Stripped to its accounting, Part II claims far less than its scope suggests, and the modesty is deliberate:

- **Forced (from the framework’s own ingredients):** that matter is fermionic (torsion is spin-sourced); that an antiparticle conjugation exists; that *if* baryons are three-soliton states, a three-valued antisymmetric internal label is required (the Pauli/Δ⁺⁺ argument); that a single soliton’s internal ladder is radiatively dark, hence that observable particles must be composites.
- **Suggestive (consistent with, but not derived from, the framework):** that composite masses are dominated by binding energy (true in real physics, inherited here for free); that the relativistic bound-state spectrum is Regge-shaped (matches hadrons, but also matches QCD’s own account); that resonances carry tunnelling widths; that the zoo is residual-force chemistry.
- **Owed (the calculations the program lives or dies by):** that the Part I bounce potential admits a stable regular soliton at all, and what its spectrum is; whether the soliton’s three-valued label has the representation content of QCD colour; and, above all, whether a fermion mass ratio can be recovered as a soliton-overlap integral rather than inserted as a Yukawa.
- **Quarantined (left honestly unexplained):** the three-generation geometric hierarchy.

The shape of the result is worth stating in one breath, in the book’s idiom: *the framework’s matter sector is fermionic by necessity, carries a forced antisymmetric label and a conjugation, and reads the particle zoo as the residual-force chemistry of dark-cored composites whose masses are mostly motion — but it inputs the gauge charges, quarantines the generations, and owes the one number (a mass as an overlap) that would turn the picture from coherent into compelling.* That is a smaller, sharper, and truer claim than “particles are micro-bounces and it is all one knob,” and it is the claim Part II should stand on.

## Computation targets (the punch-list Part IV inherits)

Stated as specifications, in dependency order, because nothing downstream computes until the first is done.

1. **The soliton itself.** Solve for a stable, regular, stationary solution of the Einstein–Cartan field equations at the binding density ρ_C — the ρ² torsion repulsion against curvature plus degeneracy pressure, as an asymmetric, anharmonic well rather than a pure harmonic floor. *Inputs:* the Part I Lagrangian, the bounce profile. *Output:* a soliton mass, radius R, and internal spectrum. *Success:* a stable bound configuration exists and its scale is computed. *This is the prerequisite for everything below.*
1. **The relative spectrum (the “hydrogen atom of the theory”).** One light soliton bound to a heavy core, relativistically (Dirac equation in the heavy core’s field — the analytically tractable unequal-mass limit). *Output:* the relative ladder and its gamma-line ratios. *Success:* the m²-spacing reproduces a real Regge slope (∼1.1 GeV⁻²) as a parameter-free ratio.
1. **Colour representation content.** Compute the soliton-pair and soliton-triple representation algebra and check whether the singlet (the confined, observable combination) coincides with the bound ground state, and whether the multiplet structure matches 3 ⊗ 3̄ = 8 ⊕ 1 and 3 ⊗ 3 ⊗ 3 ⊃ 1. *Success:* the antisymmetric three-body singlet is the baryon ground state.
1. **The headline: Yukawa as overlap.** With soliton wavefunctions in hand, compute one fermion mass (or mass ratio) as an overlap integral between bound solitons. *Success:* one ratio, parameter-free, matching observation.
1. **The density question.** Determine whether the particle-scale binding density coincides with the cosmological ρ_C ∼ 10⁵⁰ kg m⁻³ of Part I, or differs — and if it differs, by how much and why. *This closes the open caveat of the thesis section and reconciles Part II with Part I.*

*Part III (Electroweak Chemistry) builds on targets 1–3 and adds its own — the composite vector resonances, the custodial-symmetry question, and the precision-electroweak (S, T) confrontation — and must first resolve the consistency tension noted in the cover message: the Part I Hehl–Datta four-fermion term is C-, P-, and T-even, while the weak interaction is maximally C- and P-violating. Any “weak from torsion” claim has to confront that head-on, and it is flagged at the top of Part III rather than buried.*

  label *is* the SU(3) fundamental, so the two-body colour factor ⟨T₁·T₂⟩ is fixed

- **Generations are a geometric ladder, and finite.** If generations are internal rungs,

**Suggestive (confirmed in shape):** the relativistic tower is ~Regge (E² linear in rung,

**The downgrade — honest:** the effective forces, computed as soliton overlaps, all
**screen**: the one-gluon-exchange channels and the residual σ-exchange between singlets
asymptote to *zero*, not to a linear ~r. **Confinement (V~σr) is NOT produced by the
overlap picture** — it is a non-perturbative flux tube of the non-abelian connection.
Whether the gravity-torsion connection actually confines (~r) or only screens (→0) is the
deepest open question of the strong sector: if it only screens, quarks would not be
confined and the picture fails here. Lattice-scale, undecided (`color_force.py`,
`figures/pdf/color_force.pdf`). So the colour *charge* structure is forced and correct,
but the confining *force* is owed.

**Three colours or four?** Not settled, but the working answer is **three**: it is the
forced minimum (Pauli), it reproduces the observed colour-channel structure (mesons,
baryons, no free diquark), and it gives the lower S (N_c/6π). Four (Pati–Salam, lepton as
the fourth colour) is elegant — it would tie colour to the *B−L* the bounce already
conserves — but it is *not* forced and it makes S *worse* (S grows with N_c). So three is
favoured; four is a speculative extension carried at an S cost.

### Confinement update — the substrate supplies the linear term (conditional)

The downgrade above (overlaps screen; confinement ~r is not a pairwise overlap) has a
candidate resolution, computed. If the gravity–torsion vacuum is a **dual
superconductor** — its chiral condensate (the same σ the soliton forms) expelling
colour-electric flux by a dual Meissner effect — then the flux between two colour
sources is squeezed into a **Nielsen–Olesen flux tube**, a 1-D string whose energy is
proportional to its length: V(L) = σL, confinement. The vortex ODEs solve
(`flux_tube.py`); at the BPS point (m_H=m_W) the tension saturates its topological
bound, σ = 2π v² exactly — the computation's validation. So **a confining flux tube
exists, with tension σ ∝ v² = f_π²** — the *same* condensate scale that sets the
(configurational) mass also sets the confinement scale: one substrate, both jobs.

This is **conditional**: it assumes the gravity–torsion vacuum actually is a dual
superconductor (monopole condensation in the non-abelian connection). Whether it is is
the genuinely non-perturbative question, and it is a clean, well-posed **lattice** target
(the Wilson-loop area law / a dual order parameter for the Part I connection; Part IV
addendum). So confinement is now *possible and scaled*, not assumed — but whether the
substrate realises it is owed to lattice.

### How the overlap and the tube combine — one substrate, two regimes

It is worth stating how the two contributions to the inter-soliton potential relate,
because they are not two independent forces added blindly. Both come from the *same*
gravity–torsion field, as two different responses of it:

- the **overlap** is that field shared *directly* in the two solitons' tails — a genuine
  two-body wavefunction overlap (one-gluon-exchange and residual σ-exchange), short-range,
  screening to zero;
- the **tube** is that field organised by the *substrate vacuum* into a flux string
  *between* the sources — a collective response of the condensate, not a pairwise overlap,
  long-range, growing as L.

They dominate in different regimes — the overlap at short range (while the cores
interpenetrate, before a tube has formed), the tube at long range (after the overlap has
screened away) — and match smoothly between. So the practical form is the **Cornell
sum**, V(r) ≈ −κ_overlap/r + σ L, and it is legitimate *precisely because the two regimes
separate cleanly* (which is why the Cornell potential works so well in QCD). But the right
picture is one field with two limits — a perturbative (overlap) limit and a
non-perturbative (substrate/tube) limit — not two forces. The overlap *dresses*; the
substrate *confines*. The overlap builds the short-distance, "atomic" part of the hadron
spectrum; the tube builds the long-distance, Regge/string part.
